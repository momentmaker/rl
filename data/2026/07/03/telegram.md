*Random Learning - 3 July 2026*

Three things I went down the rabbit hole on today.

*1. Why people quit Anki (it's math, not willpower)*
The number one reason people abandon spaced repetition isn't laziness - it's the "Anki death spiral." You start hot with 50+ new cards a day, miss a day, come back to 200+ reviews due, feel punished, and quit. The survivors do boring things: cap new cards around 20/day, always clear reviews before adding new ones, and treat a "leech" card as a broken card to rewrite (one fact, short) rather than something to grind through. Bonus: the old "ease hell" problem - where hard cards got permanently punished and kept resurfacing - was largely killed by the new FSRS algorithm, which now cuts reviews 20-30% at the same retention. The new failure mode is over-optimizing FSRS instead of just doing your reviews.

*2. Is the AI boom a bubble? Follow the circular money.*
The chart everyone's drawing: Nvidia put ~$40B into AI equity this year (~$30B into OpenAI, now its biggest customer), OpenAI commits ~$75B/year to Oracle, and Oracle fills its datacenters with Nvidia chips. Bloomberg counts $800B+ of these interlocking deals. Ed Zitron's viral bear take: strip out the circular financing and real external AI demand is maybe $1-2B. The sharper skeptics aren't doomers though - their line is "the bull case can be right and still wreck you on timing": ~$1.4T of compute commitments vs ~$20B revenue, leverage hidden in private credit. The historical rhyme isn't pets.com - it's 1990s telecom vendor financing (Lucent/Nortel lending customers the money to buy their gear), which "grew, right up until it didn't." The weakest link is the "neoclouds," whose revenue lags capex 2:1.

*3. The em dash didn't do anything wrong*
The em dash ( — ) became AI's telltale signature, and the reason is boring: skilled human writers use it constantly, so it saturated the training data, so the model reaches for it. A scrape of ~90,000 Reddit posts crowned it the #1 AI "tell" - but noted the *reliable* tells are the ones software can't fake: flat sentence rhythm and a "constant, unnatural positivity." The sad twist: real humans are now stripping a centuries-old mark (Dickinson to Didion) from their writing to avoid being called fake. And the detectors barely work - ~87% accurate on raw AI text, dropping to ~62% once a human edits it, worse on Claude/Gemini than GPT. The reframe that resolves it: the stigma isn't on the punctuation, it's on AI writing. The em dash is just a convenient scapegoat.
