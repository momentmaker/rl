*Random Learning — 8 July 2026*

Three things I went down the rabbit hole on today.

*1. AI bug-hunting vs the slop flood — and the maintainer in the middle*
Two AI curves crossed over open source this year and they point opposite ways. On one side, agents got genuinely good at finding real bugs: Google's Big Sleep turned up 20 unknown vulns in widely-used OSS and caught a real SQLite CVE before attackers could; AISLE reportedly hit 12 of 12 OpenSSL zero-days; XBOW became the first non-human to top HackerOne's US leaderboard. On the other side, the same cheap generation turned bug-bounty inboxes into landfill — Daniel Stenberg killed curl's bounty in early 2026 after AI slop swamped the team (roughly 20% of 2025 reports were outright slop, only ~5% genuine; seven bogus reports in one 16-hour stretch), reopened it, then just declared July a no-reports "summer of bliss." The structural insight everyone landed on: AI collapsed the cost of *finding* bugs and did nothing for the cost of *verifying* them, so the human bottleneck — the unpaid, often solo maintainer — gets crushed from both sides. The fix isn't "ban AI reports" (the real finds are too valuable); it's shifting cost back to the submitter: proof-of-concept required, reputation/stake gates, AI triage as a first-pass filter.

*2. Local coding LLMs people actually run in 2026*
They finally crossed from "cute demo" to "useful for the boring 70%." The model most people run is Qwen3-Coder-30B-A3B — a 30B mixture-of-experts with only ~3B active, so it fits a 32-48GB machine and moves fast, especially now that Ollama ships an MLX backend (~130 tok/s on a Mac mini M4 Pro vs ~43 on the old llama.cpp path). Reality check from r/LocalLLaMA: these are within a few benchmark points of frontier on well-defined tasks but still cost 1-3 corrections a run and aren't a Claude substitute. The clinic in what "local" costs is antirez (Redis creator), who hand-wrote a C+Metal engine for DeepSeek V4 Flash (284B MoE): a 2-bit quant matched full precision on his vuln-finding tests, but only ~26 tok/s on a maxed 128GB MacBook — "interesting for experiments, not for daily use in coding agents." A correction worth knowing: the Mac mini M4 tops out at 48GB (the 128GB figure is a Studio). The settled workflow is hybrid — route the easy majority local for privacy and free-at-the-margin cost, escalate the hard, novel, or repo-spanning 20-40% to a frontier API.

*3. Organic Maps forked into CoMaps — and why*
The offline-OSM app everyone recommended never broke; the community running it did. In April 2025 contributors published an open letter accusing the shareholders of the for-profit Organic Maps OÜ of taking donations into a company with closed books, striking a Kayak deal behind closed doors, and resisting open-sourcing the metaserver. When talks stalled, they forked to CoMaps — a not-for-profit, community-governed, zero-proprietary rebuild that decided its own name and roadmap by public vote. It shipped to F-Droid in June 2025 and the app stores that July, and the trust signal is loud: privacy distros iodéOS (2025) and CalyxOS (May 2026) swapped their default from Organic Maps to CoMaps. Organic Maps isn't abandoned and switching is low-stakes (same OpenStreetMap data underneath), but there's no upside to staying on the version whose donations and trademark sit inside a stalled shareholder dispute. Practical read: install CoMaps; keep OsmAnd around as the power-user heavy-lifter.
