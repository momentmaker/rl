📚 3 rabbit holes today

🖥️ The Mac mini as an always-on headless home server
The small Apple-silicon box quietly became the default 24/7 home server in 2026, and the driver is AI agents — Apple's own silicon exec told MacRumors the mini and Studio are now the "machines of choice" for running them. The whole game is remote access to a "headless" box (no monitor/keyboard/mouse): people reach for Tailscale (mesh VPN) + SSH + Screen Sharing, or a turnkey remote-desktop app. Recurring gotcha: macOS wants a display present, so an HDMI dummy plug keeps screen-sharing awake. What runs on it splits two ways — classic home-server duties (NAS, an Immich/Google-Photos alternative, Jellyfin, smart-home hub) and an unattended AI worker (local LLMs via Ollama/LM Studio/MLX, or a `claude -p` cron worker reaching LAN services). "Always-on" is really a power/wake discipline: auto-restart after outage, wake-on-network, no deep sleep — affordable thanks to single-digit idle watts.

🧪 The online malware sandboxes people actually use — and their traps
Security folks spent the month warning against the reflex "just drop it in VirusTotal." The sharpest line: "0 detections ≠ safe" — sandbox-evasive and brand-new payloads slip past automation, so a clean scan means the scanners missed it, not that the file is safe. The working roster is VirusTotal (multi-engine first look) + Hybrid Analysis / ANY.RUN / Joe Sandbox (detonate the file in an instrumented VM) + URLScan.io for links, used as triage rather than a verdict. 2026's shift: one-command and agent-driven triage — CLIs like Malwoverview query 15+ threat-intel platforms at once, and LLMs now call sandboxes + IOC feeds (MalwareBazaar, ThreatFox, URLhaus) as tools. The mirror problem is false positives — clean installers and unsigned binaries over-trigger engines — so confirm with a second independent method. And never upload sensitive internal files: public sandboxes often share samples with researchers.

📱 Quishing (QR-code phishing) now gets past MFA
Quishing is just phishing wearing a QR code, and it works for two structural reasons: the code is an image, so it sails past email URL filters, and scanning hops you onto your personal phone, off the managed device. Two flavors: QR-in-email login lures (fake "Microsoft 365 / IT wants you to re-verify") and physical sticker overlays pasted over the real code on parking meters, restaurant tables, and payment machines. The 2026 escalation is nasty — AI drove a reported 14x phishing jump, and Google's June advisory flagged AITM quishing that proxies the real login *including the MFA challenge* to steal your live session cookie, so completing MFA doesn't save you. Clone-site + fake-QR kits sell for under $50; China's "ScanScam" (public QR codes redirected) shows it in the wild. The fixes are behavioral and near-free: preview the URL before opening, never pay or log in from a scanned sticker (use the official app), check for overlay stickers, and distrust unexpected QR codes in email.
