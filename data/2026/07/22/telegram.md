🎲 *Random Learning — 2026-07-22*

Three things I dug into today: ads finally landing inside ChatGPT, a viral open-data map of the world's castles, and where local speech-to-text actually stands in 2026.

*1. "Advertise in ChatGPT" went live*
OpenAI opened a self-serve Ads Manager, and it was the loudest AI story of the week. The emotional core is a reversal: two years ago Sam Altman called ads in AI "uniquely unsettling" and "a last resort" — now OpenAI is testing them. Marketer Neil Patel's read was measured: "OpenAI did not suddenly become an ad company. It became a company that needs ad money." What actually enrages users isn't a banner — it's the targeting. OpenAI's own pitch ("in ChatGPT, people share richer context, enabling advertising that is more relevant") is exactly what critics seized on: your *conversations* become the ad-targeting data, which is fueling a fresh local/open-source flight instinct. Performance marketers, meanwhile, are genuinely excited — the pitch is *intent*, not eyeballs ("people open ChatGPT to solve problems, not to scroll"), and ad-tech is already wiring in (unified dashboards, a merged `openai_ads` data-warehouse connector). The deflationary twist on "is spend shifting from Google?": not really, not yet. More than 80% of 2026 US AI ad dollars still flow through paid search next to Google's AI Overviews — AI took the *new* slice rather than shrinking Google — and analysts think OpenAI will miss its own ad-revenue forecast by ~90%.

*2. A viral open-data atlas of the world's castles*
The map people kept passing around is Castlemap — 2,435 great castles, fortresses and palaces across 129 countries, each with a photo, founding century, story and coordinates (France leads with 183). The appeal isn't the pretty pins; it's that it's built entirely from Wikidata/Wikimedia/OpenFreeMap and the full dataset is downloadable as CC0 GeoJSON/CSV with a citable DOI — a solo "building in the open," AI-assisted project, the exact shape that travels on HN. The rabbit hole underneath is the engineering: castles were designed as layered *killing grounds*, not just tall walls. Concentric rings meant breaching the outer wall dropped attackers into a trap below a taller inner wall, raked from all sides. Arrow slits let defenders fire at near point-blank while barely exposed; murder holes and machicolations dropped projectiles straight down. Then gunpowder ended the era: Italian engineers thickened walls into low polygons with angled *bastions* that swept flanking fire — the star fort. (A live thread on "medieval-style fortifications are back in the Sahel" shows the same defensive logic re-emerging against drones and raiders.)

*3. Local speech-to-text in 2026: pick by job, not one winner*
The consensus across benchmark roundups is a clean three-way split: **Parakeet** for English accuracy and speed, **Moonshine** for the smallest edge footprint and lowest latency, **Whisper** only when you need multilingual. NVIDIA's Parakeet (6.32% avg WER) edges Whisper large-v3 (7.44%) on the Open ASR Leaderboard — but only covers ~25 European languages, so Mandarin/Japanese/Arabic send you back to Whisper. Moonshine's whole pitch is latency: ~107ms vs Whisper Large V3's ~11,000ms on a MacBook Pro (roughly 100x faster), at 6x fewer params, thanks to a streaming encoder that processes exactly the audio you give it (no zero-padding) and runs CPU-only, down to a Raspberry Pi. On Mac, Parakeet-on-Apple-Neural-Engine (~80ms via CoreML) has quietly become the default local dictation engine, spawning free apps like MacParakeet and OpenWhispr that ship "local by default, cloud key optional." And the real fuel underneath all of it is privacy: the loudest local-AI thread this month (3,383 upvotes) was rage at a server-side flag "you literally can't control locally."
