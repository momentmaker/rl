*Random Learning — 2026-07-02*

Three things I dug into today, from the last 30 days of what people are saying.

*1. Porsche's eFuels — a lifeline for combustion, or an expensive detour?*
The debate quietly flipped this year from "will e-fuels save the engine?" to "does anyone still need them to?" The wall itself moved: in December 2025 the EU dropped its 100% combustion ban for a 90% CO2-cut target by 2035, letting carmakers close the gap with e-fuels, biofuels or low-carbon steel — so synthetic fuel is now just one exemption lane among several. And the efficiency case against it is settled: an e-fuel car runs at ~13–15% well-to-wheel efficiency vs ~69% for a battery EV, which is why even the European Commission is pushing e-fuels toward aviation and heavy transport, not daily drivers. The freshest news beat is that surging EV sales are draining the political urgency from the whole fight. In the live social conversation it reads less like a climate debate and more like a subsidy scandal — German taxpayers calling the Porsche-in-Chile pilot plant burned public money. Honest ceiling: motorsport and classic cars, where the chemistry genuinely works.

*2. Why family businesses die by the 3rd generation — and how Japan beats it.*
The "shirtsleeves to shirtsleeves in three generations" curse is real as a pattern but softer than the proverb: ~30% of family firms reach gen 2, ~12% reach gen 3, ~3% reach gen 4. The key reframe — the third generation doesn't cause the failure, it inherits the unpaid governance debt of the first two: succession conversations nobody had, wealth left dangerously concentrated in one business. Japan flips the whole playbook. It has 33,000+ shinise (companies over 100 years old), some past 1,000, and their house codes mandate the opposite of growth-at-all-costs: stick to the core, hold up to two years of cash, avoid debt. Their succession hack is mukoyoshi — adopting a capable adult son-in-law or employee as heir rather than forcing it on an uninterested birth son; a landmark study found adopted-heir firms outperform blood-heir ones. The irony: Kongo Gumi, a temple-builder that survived 1,428 years, died in 2006 — not from a weak heir, but from 1980s bubble-era real-estate debt. It died the moment it abandoned the shinise rulebook.

*3. Most "protected" oceans are "paper parks."*
The consensus this month, crystallized at the Our Ocean Conference in Kenya: area is the vanity metric, enforcement is the one that recovers fish. ~10% of the ocean sits inside a marine protected area on paper — but only ~3.5% is actually fully protected, and the gap is the whole story. The viral proof point: bottom trawling is still legal in ~90% of the UK's "protected" waters — "protected on paper, bulldozed in the water." What actually works is no-take zones plus real enforcement plus local buy-in: Mexico's Cabo Pulmo saw fish biomass rebound 463% after the community switched from fishing to guarding the reef. The cautionary flip side — an Indonesian no-take zone where biomass fell >50% because nobody patrolled it; a line on a map with no guards behaves like open water. The named fix for 2026 isn't more boundaries, it's durable financing (a new $10M Marine 30x30 finance initiative) to pay for the enforcement that turns paper parks into real ones.
