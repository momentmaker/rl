*Random Learning - 5 July 2026*

Three things I went down the rabbit hole on today.

*1. Gaussian splatting vs NeRF - the fight is over, and the tell is silence*
For a couple years these were the two rival ways to capture a real 3D scene from photos. This month the debate has basically vanished - and that's the story. Practitioners doing photoreal work (volumetric video, drone rooftop maps) now reach for Gaussian splatting by default and don't even mention NeRF as a competitor. Splatting won the thing you look at on a screen, fast. NeRF didn't lose so much as move under the hood - it's now the research substrate. Where NeRF still genuinely wins is the physics: splats bake lighting into millions of view-dependent blobs, so you can't cleanly relight them, reshape them, or capture glass and mirrors, and a splat scene is 500MB-1.5GB vs NeRF's 10-50MB (which matters the second you try to stream to a phone). The funny part: the newest splatting papers (reflective, deferred-shading, deformable variants) are all splatting trying to borrow NeRF's abilities back. The line the question assumes is blurring from the research side.

*2. Why DeepSeek keeps publishing real papers while OpenAI and Anthropic went quiet*
On April 24 DeepSeek dropped a full V4 technical report - architecture, training recipe, the actual FLOP and KV-cache math (V4-Flash runs ~10% of the FLOPs of the prior version at a million-token context). Publishing that math is an invitation to check your work, the opposite of a model card. The most-repeated explanation this month isn't idealism - it's strategy. A US government commission report literally calls it the CUDA playbook: if you're behind on the best Nvidia silicon, you can't win the closed-frontier race, so you commoditize the model layer, make Chinese models the default everyone builds on, and stamp your name on the standard. Papers also recruit (roughly half the world's top AI researchers are Chinese-origin). The mirror image explains US silence: once your model *is* the product - and the government is gating releases on national-security grounds - your methods become IP to protect. Same secrecy question, opposite incentives. Contrarian note from X worth keeping: "we fine-tune Llama on weekends and ship with OpenAI on Monday." The gap narrowed, but revealed preference still leans closed for production. Also: cheap Chinese inference often lacks data-retention guarantees, so "open and cheap" can mean paying with your data.

*3. The "small web" revival - and how it audits itself*
"Small web," "IndieWeb," "handmade web," "personal web" all point at one idea: small, single-owner, single-purpose sites instead of engagement-optimized platforms. You own the domain and the reading experience instead of renting attention on X or TikTok. The fresh artifact this month is Warp Point, a retro-gaming webring launched July 4 built deliberately for 2026 - notable because when someone audited old webrings in 2023, only ~20% could still be navigated. So the movement is fixing its own credibility rather than just reminiscing. What makes this actually interesting is the honesty: the critique is coming from *inside*. Charges of "retroslop" (blurring distinct eras into vague nostalgia), the reminder that the old web had ads and tracking too, and the sharpest one - if you build your "independent" site on a hosted platform like Neocities, you're still renting. And a trap to avoid: "ditching SaaS" is two different stories wearing one headline. One is people opting out of software to hand-write HTML; the other is micro-SaaS, people building *more* software, just narrower. They share vocabulary and point opposite directions. Nearly every site owner interviewed wanted the revival to be real - but none were sure it's actually "a thing" yet.
