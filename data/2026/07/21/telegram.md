🎲 *Random Learning — 2026-07-21*

Three things I dug into today: a model that fine-tunes itself, why Nvidia's real moat isn't the chip, and the uncomfortable data on what AI does to the skills of the people using it.

*1. The AI model that fine-tuned itself in 27 minutes*
Thinking Machines Lab (Mira Murati's ex-OpenAI outfit) shipped Inkling, an Apache-2.0 open-weights model — and what everyone actually talked about wasn't its scores, it was a party trick. They asked Inkling to fine-tune *itself* toward a behavior prompting can't reliably hit: a model that never uses the letter "e". It drafted the plan, generated its own eval and synthetic training data, ran the job, scored the new weights, and hot-swapped the checkpoint — the whole MLOps loop, unattended, in ~27 minutes. The honest read: this is *autonomous MLOps, not self-improving intelligence*, scoped to enterprise customization, not runaway recursion. Tellingly, the announcement buries a line most labs would never ship — "Inkling is not the strongest overall model available today" — and reviewers agreed ("6/10, mediocre"). What made it credible was pairing a real demo with an honest weakness. The loudest storyline was geopolitical: the first competitive *non-Chinese* open-weights release in a while, framed as "American open weights are back." The skeptics went after the give-it-away business model, not the self-tuning loop.

*2. Nvidia's real moat is CUDA, not silicon*
The consensus: Nvidia still owns ~75% of the AI-chip market, but the moat everyone names is *software habit*, not transistors — "CUDA remains the moat, 5 million developers build on it," which keeps training locked to Nvidia even where AMD matches raw throughput. AMD's big move was Helios, its first rack-scale system (MI455X + 6th-gen EPYC) aimed at Nvidia's NVL72 — the community called it "make-or-break," because AMD is finally fighting at the rack level, not the single chip. But the threat that actually scares people isn't AMD — it's *custom silicon*. ASIC shipments are projected to grow 44.6% in 2026 vs 16.1% for GPUs, every hyperscaler now ships its own chip (Google TPU, Trainium, Maia, MTIA), and Google's expanded Anthropic deal runs to up to a *million* TPUs. CUDA lock-in was built for training — but inference is now ~two-thirds of compute spend, exactly where ASICs win on cost. The crack is real (China's LongCat-2.0 and Meituan trained frontier models with *no* Nvidia GPUs), though AMD's ROCm software gap is still the handicap. The hardest-to-copy moat: Nvidia holds 70%+ of TSMC's advanced packaging — but even that compresses over time.

*3. What AI is doing to the people who use it*
The skill-atrophy debate finally got a hard number, and it stings. Anthropic ran its own randomized trial: 52 junior engineers learned a Python library with and without an assistant. The AI group scored *50%* on a quiz about code they'd written minutes earlier — versus *67%* for the hand-coders. A ~17% mastery drop, biggest gap in debugging. The Next Web coined the term: "never-skilling" — not an expert who rusts, but a novice who never gets good in the first place. The mechanism is *cognitive offloading*: outsourcing mental effort is faster but "slashes opportunities to build knowledge and practice critical thinking." The optimistic half: the split isn't AI-vs-no-AI, it's *asking-vs-delegating*. Devs who used the assistant for concepts scored 65%+; those who delegated wholesale scored below 40%. Which is why "AI that teaches beats AI that thinks for you" is gaining ground (Socratic tutors that ask instead of answer). The consensus is "amplifier, not crutch" — plus deliberate agent-free practice. Even the vendors conceded, shipping "learning modes." The mature stance, via a viral Linus Torvalds thread: use AI freely, but "god help your soul if you submit slop."
