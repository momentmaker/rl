*Random Learning — 6 July 2026*

Three things I went down the rabbit hole on today.

*1. Bambu Lab locked down its 3D printers — and the maker world revolted*
Bambu makes the printers that finally made 3D printing "just work," but in 2026 it added cloud authentication, blocked third-party slicers, and routed control through middleware called Bambu Connect. The community's shared metaphor: it "pulled the HP playbook" — build on an open ecosystem, then fence it. Jeff Geerling's line, "Bambu Lab is abusing the open source social contract," is the sentence the whole argument hangs on. It escalated: Bambu sent a cease-and-desist to the Polish maintainer of an AGPL-licensed OrcaSlicer fork that restored the control the firmware had stripped — a company using AGPL code to threaten someone for using AGPL code. He took the project down rather than fight; Louis Rossmann pledged $10K for his defense, and the Software Freedom Conservancy opened a probe that confirmed two AGPLv3 violations *by Bambu*. The twist that undercuts the outrage: cutting a Bambu off the cloud "loses exactly two features," so the anger is about ownership as principle, not broken machines — 89% of owners still rate it 4+ stars. And the "do open printers still matter?" question answered itself. Prusa (which grew straight out of RepRap) shipped a speed-matched CORE One, released full CAD under a new Open Community License "aimed at addressing industry abuses," and became the open printer that finally keeps up.

*2. Why once-great brands turn to junk after private equity buys them*
The most-shared framing this month was a developer thread listing every product that "has gotten shitty," and the comments converged on one culprit: ownership that optimizes for extraction over the thing itself. Cory Doctorow's essay on "private equity's looting" gave the crowd its vocabulary — leveraged buyout, debt dump, asset strip, dividend recap — and tied it to the "enshittification" everyone already feels. The playbook people recite is the real mechanism: buy a healthy brand with borrowed money, load the *brand* with that debt, cut quality to service it, extract dividends, walk away when it collapses. Red Lobster (Chapter 11 in 2024 after Golden Gate piled on debt), Instant Pot (Cornell Capital saddled a beloved product with ~$450M in debt, bankrupt anyway), Toys R Us, Sears, Payless, Claire's — the same movie. And 2026 keeps handing the argument fresh corpses: Saks filed in January carrying $3B+ in debt. Where it hits the grocery aisle is shrinkflation — snacks, candy, and soda are both the categories most associated with it and the most abandoned. It's converting to behavior: ~49% of shoppers say they'd switch after noticing shrinkflation, ~35% move to store brands. Even Wall Street voices now concede PE's reputation has curdled enough to damage the brands it buys.

*3. Phantom islands — places mapped for centuries that never existed*
The genre's folklore anchor is Hy-Brasil, which per Irish myth appears off Ireland's Atlantic coast for one day every seven years — and 2026 is a "seventh year," which gave it a fresh hook this month. It sat on Genoese charts from 1325 and survived on 300+ maps for nearly seven centuries; last "sighting" logged 1872. But the stories people love most have a precise death date. Sandy Island, near New Caledonia, was charted from the 1800s (Captain Cook noted it) and rode the digital World Vector Shoreline Database all the way onto Google Maps — until a research vessel sailed straight through in 2012 and found 4,620 feet of open ocean. It survived that long *because* everyone copied the same database when paper charts went digital. The darkest one is Bermeja, an islet off the Yucatán last charted in 1941; a 2009 survey found nothing there. Because it sat near a Gulf oil zone estimated at 22.5 billion barrels, its absence cost Mexico maritime rights — and Mexican senators openly floated that the CIA had blown it up. No evidence, but it's the version that travels: a map error with an oil war attached.
