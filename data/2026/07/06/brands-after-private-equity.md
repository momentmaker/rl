What I learned:

**"Everything I use has gotten shitty" is the mood, and people are naming the mechanism** - The most-shared framing this month came from developer [@championswimmer](https://x.com/championswimmer/status/2069962641204707730), whose thread (1,057 likes) listing every once-careful product that "has gotten shitty" struck a nerve well beyond software. The comments converge on a single culprit: ownership that optimizes for extraction over the thing itself. Cory Doctorow's widely-passed-around essay [The long, bloody lineage of private equity's looting](https://doctorow.medium.com/the-long-bloody-lineage-of-private-equitys-looting-798597a4fa30) gave the crowd its vocabulary - the leveraged buyout, the debt dump, the asset strip, the dividend recap - and tied it directly to the "enshittification" everyone already feels.

**The playbook people keep describing is real and repeatable, not a vibe** - The pattern shoppers recite is the actual PE mechanism: buy a healthy brand with borrowed money, load the brand itself with the debt, cut costs and quality to service it, extract dividends, then walk away when it collapses. [Private Banker International](https://www.privatebankerinternational.com/features/how-private-equity-keeps-failing-brands-like-red-lobster/) lays it out through Red Lobster, run into Chapter 11 in 2024 after Golden Gate Capital piled on debt and rent. [Civic Skunk Works](https://medium.com/civic-skunk-works/gone-in-an-instant-1f5c1965ebba) does the same for Instant Pot: Cornell Capital saddled a profitable, beloved product with roughly $450M in debt and it went bankrupt anyway. Toys R Us, Sears, Payless, RadioShack, Joann, 99 Cents Only, Claire's - the same movie, over and over.

**2026 keeps handing the argument fresh corpses** - The story has stayed hot because the bankruptcies keep landing. Per the [PE Stakeholder Project](https://pestakeholder.org/news/private-equity-buyout-tracker-q1-2026/), Saks Fifth Avenue filed in the first two weeks of 2026 carrying over $3 billion in debt, and Claire's - bought by Apollo in 2007 in a buyout that loaded it with $2.5 billion - filed for the second time in August 2025. Every new headline reloads the "PE ruins brands" thesis with a name people recognize.

**Shrinkflation is where the abstract grievance becomes a grocery aisle** - When shoppers name what they've stopped trusting, they point at the shelf. [FoodNavigator](https://www.foodnavigator.com/Article/2025/09/22/shrinkflation-backfires-brands-risk-customer-loyalty-in-cost-saving-strategy/) reports confectionery, snacks, and soft drinks are both the categories most associated with shrinkflation and the ones most vulnerable to abandonment. Reddit's raw feeling shows up in threads like [r/mildlyinfuriating](https://www.reddit.com/r/mildlyinfuriating/comments/1uol4e6/expectations_vs_reality/) "Expectations vs reality" (19.5K upvotes) - the "you're paying more for less and hiding it" reaction that dominates the mood without needing a citation.

**The trust is migrating to store brands, and the data backs the exodus** - This is the concrete shopper behavior underneath the anger. Per consumer research from [aytm](https://aytm.com/post/shrinkflation-skimpflation-spaving), 48.8% of shoppers say they'd switch brands after noticing shrinkflation and 34.5% intend to buy the store-brand or generic instead. Categories once seen as safe branded territory - snacks, soda, household paper, cleaning products - are exactly where private label is now taking share. The distrust isn't just venting; it's showing up as defection.

**Even Wall Street voices concede the reputational cost** - It's not only angry consumers. [@WallStreetMav](https://x.com/WallStreetMav) and finance-side commentators are acknowledging that PE's public reputation has curdled to the point it can damage the brands it buys - a notable shift when the extraction critique starts coming from inside the tent. Meanwhile [Polymarket](https://polymarket.com/) traders are pricing a narrow food-industry question (ConAgra earnings call at 92%), a small reminder that the packaged-food giants living under this scrutiny are the same ones shoppers are walking away from.

KEY PATTERNS from the research:
1. The grievance names a specific mechanism (LBO, debt-load, asset-strip, dividend recap), not a vague "corporate greed" - per [@championswimmer](https://x.com/championswimmer/status/2069962641204707730)
2. Doctorow's "looting" framing is the essay giving the crowd its shared vocabulary and linking PE to enshittification - per [doctorow.medium.com](https://doctorow.medium.com/the-long-bloody-lineage-of-private-equitys-looting-798597a4fa30)
3. The case list is remarkably stable across sources: Red Lobster, Instant Pot, Toys R Us, Sears, Claire's, Saks - per [pestakeholder.org](https://pestakeholder.org/news/private-equity-buyout-tracker-q1-2026/)
4. Shoppers translate the abstract critique into a concrete complaint: shrinkflation in snacks, candy, and soda - per [foodnavigator.com](https://www.foodnavigator.com/Article/2025/09/22/shrinkflation-backfires-brands-risk-customer-loyalty-in-cost-saving-strategy/)
5. Distrust is converting to behavior: ~49% would switch, ~35% move to store brands - per [aytm.com](https://aytm.com/post/shrinkflation-skimpflation-spaving)
