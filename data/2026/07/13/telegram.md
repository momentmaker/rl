📚 3 rabbit holes today

🔊 How brands design their "sonic identity" (and why Netflix's ta-dum is a wedding ring)
That two-note Netflix "ta-dum" is a found sound: Oscar-winning editor Lon Bender knocked his wedding ring against a bedroom cabinet, and another designer layered in a reversed electric-guitar swell made with 90s effects pedals. It took a year, and a finalist version ended in a goat bleat before that got scrapped. The craft lesson: iconic audio logos are physical performance and found objects, not generated tones. Sonic branding is a small, named field - MassiveMusic built TikTok's sound, Made Music Studio is behind AT&T and the modern HBO refresh, and Intel's four-note bong was written by Walter Werzowa. The benchmark case is Mastercard, whose sonic brand is a "10-layer" system built on one lyric-less melody that flexes into opera, EDM, and regional styles so it stays recognizable everywhere. The 2026 surge driver is screenless, voice-first interfaces (smart speakers, cars, wearables) where there's no logo to show - reportedly 139 of the top 250 global brands now hold formal sonic identities. On AI, practitioners are unusually settled: it's a production assistant that scales a human-composed core into thousands of on-brand micro-assets, not the composer - partly because "technically correct isn't emotionally right," and partly because AI-only works can't be copyrighted, so an AI-generated audio logo is legally unprotectable.

🧠 "Taste" is the AI-era skill nobody can define
"Taste is the new moat" is everywhere this month - and even boosters admit they can't define it (one called it a Minsky "suitcase word"). The useful move is to make it concrete. Engineers describe taste as calibration across dozens of interacting dials - pattern recognition applied to quality. The clearest working boundary: AI can build the thing, but it can't tell you whether the thing should exist or whether it fits the system. The sharpest dissent comes from Shrivu Shankar's "Taste Is Not a Moat" - your judgment is only valuable relative to what AI produces by default, and that default keeps improving, so taste is decaying alpha, not a durable moat. What actually separates good output from slop? Addy Osmani's answer: architectural fit + explainability - "if you can't explain your change, you don't understand it well enough to ask someone to approve it." The scariest signal in the wild is a top thread arguing the real harm of AI tools isn't job loss - it's mid-level engineers who can no longer explain what they built. You build the judgment by reading great code and postmortems, treating rejected drafts as a compass, and turning each taste decision into a deterministic check.

🎬 Real video editors that run entirely in your browser
The 2026 pattern for in-browser video isn't one tech winning - it's a hybrid. Editors pair the WebCodecs API (which binds straight to the browser's hardware encoder/decoder - the same silicon Chrome uses to play YouTube) for fast playback and scrubbing, with ffmpeg.wasm as the software fallback for odd formats. The catch everyone hits: WebCodecs has zero file I/O - it only converts encoded chunks to and from raw frames, so you must bolt on a muxer/demuxer. The repo the scene is converging on is Mediabunny (6.7K stars, pure TypeScript); even Remotion is migrating onto it. Browser support is finally real - AV1 + HEVC cover 99.7% of sessions for decode - but jagged at the codec level (HEVC is basically Safari-only, AV1 is everyone-but-Safari, AAC encode breaks on Firefox and Linux), so you feature-detect per codec with isConfigSupported(). Why eat the complexity? Privacy: client-side encode means the footage never leaves the device - the pitch pulling users off CapCut now that it requires an account and uploads your clips.
