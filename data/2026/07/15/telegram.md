📚 3 rabbit holes today

🔋 Does recycling EV batteries actually pay off in 2026?
The answer splits entirely on chemistry, and that's the whole story. Nickel-rich NMC/NCA packs still pay to recycle; the cheaper LFP chemistry that took over the market carries so little recoverable value that recyclers have to charge on both ends to break even - Cirba Solutions' CEO put it as "there's really no value in recycling iron phosphate." The cautionary tale is Li-Cycle, whose keystone Rochester Hub ballooned from $560M toward $1B, never opened, and tipped into bankruptcy; the twist is that Glencore quietly credit-bid the assets for ~$40M and now plans to restart it - a commodity giant succeeding where a pure-play startup couldn't. Ascend Elements filed Chapter 11 too. The survivors changed the business they're in: Redwood Materials raised a $425M "Redwood Energy" round (Google, Nvidia) to turn used packs into grid storage for AI data centers rather than shred everything. Japan's genuine tech breakthrough - JX Metals hitting 90% lithium recovery vs the sub-50% norm - is real but starved for feedstock: only ~14% of dead batteries reach official collection, and EV packs last 12-15 years, so the waste stream doesn't peak until after 2040. Plants built now are fighting over thin scrap.

🚕 Robotaxis, ride-comparison apps, and who's actually getting undercut
The robot is no longer the expensive option. Waymo's premium over Uber collapsed from 30-40% six months ago to ~13%, and vanishes on longer trips once you count that nobody tips a robot. Tesla showed up and undercut everyone - roughly half Waymo's fare on the same route ($6.15 vs $13.93 on one 2.25-mile trip), the classic Uber loss-leader playbook. Drivers feel it: Atlanta rideshare drivers rallied on July 10 saying Waymo is skimming their most profitable short trips, with income down "10 to 30%," and r/uberdrivers ran a 553-upvote thread simply titled "Uber is dead." But the undercut narrative is genuinely contested - rider volume is reportedly growing fastest in the same cities robotaxis launched, and a lot of driver anger targets tipping and their own platforms as much as the robots. The new consumer wedge is ride-comparison apps like Hackney (Show HN), which sign into your own accounts to show true personalized prices across Uber, Lyft, Waymo and Curb - a direct answer to Consumer Reports finding Uber/Lyft show different riders ~50%-different fares for the same trip minutes apart.

📖 The craft (and collecting) of great opening lines in fiction
The spark this month is Verba Prima, a "Show HN" site that surfaces one famous literary opening line a day - it hit the front page with 169 points and 98 comments. It joins a crowded lineage: greatopeninglines.com and the canonical crowdsourced list, American Book Review's "100 Best First Lines from Novels." The craft consensus writers keep circling is that a great opener does four jobs at once - establish voice, plant an image, hint at character, and raise a question you need answered - via reusable moves like the strange detail (Orwell's clocks "striking thirteen"), stating the theme up front (Anna Karenina's unhappy families), or locking in POV voice. Standard warnings: don't open on dialogue or the weather. The deeper insight into why the same handful of lines get quoted forever: the durable ones detach into reusable idioms - "It is a truth universally acknowledged" outlives its novel because it works as a plug-in template, whereas "Call me Ishmael," though more famous, is far less reusable. There's a live contrarian camp too (Dappled Things, the Irish Times' "Call me Ishmael, at your peril"): a first line is only beautiful after you've read the whole book, so celebrating it in isolation - and ranking sentences out of context - is backwards. The debate lives in general book/writing subreddits (r/books, r/writing), not a niche hub - it's a steady evergreen conversation, not a viral spike.
