*Random Learning — 2026-07-01*

Three things I dug into today, from the last 30 days of what people are saying.

*1. Why mugicha (barley tea) is what Japan actually drinks all summer.*
The mental model outsiders miss: mugicha isn't a tea you choose over water — for many households it *is* the water, "consumed at nearly the same rate as water." The two-part logic is why it beats the alternatives: it carries potassium and magnesium, so it rehydrates better than plain water when you're sweating through a humid summer, and because barley is a grain (not a Camellia leaf) it has zero caffeine — which is why it's the default drink handed to babies and small kids in the heat. The emotional pull is nostalgia, not health: a glass pitcher in the fridge, ice cubes, children home from outside. The prep is deliberately foolproof — drop a barley tea bag in a pitcher of cold water, refrigerate 4–8 hours, done; it won't even turn bitter overnight. It's institutionalized enough to have a holiday (June 1 is "Mugicha Day," since 1986) and to poll as the #1 summer drink for 40%+ of Japanese respondents.

*2. Why almost everyone thinks they're a rare outlier.*
It surfaces every time a "how normal are you" tool goes viral — the canonical one is Tijmen Schep's *How Normal Am I?*, where an AI scores your face on beauty, age and BMI in the browser (it's secretly a trojan horse teaching you the "objective" score comes from a biased dataset). The reliable reaction is people treating an average result as an insult or a glitch, and newer sites weaponize it directly — one titles its results page "Are You Above Average?" The psychology is two mirror-image biases colliding: illusory superiority (the Lake Wobegon effect — 90%+ of people overrate themselves, 65% of Americans think they're smarter than average) plus the false-uniqueness effect (underestimating how many others share your traits). Together they guarantee the "surely I'm not average" gut reaction. The twist: it's culture-bound — strong in North America, near-absent in Japan/Korea/China, and depressed people rate themselves *more* accurately (depressive realism).

*3. Precious Plastic is nearly broke — is home plastic recycling worth it?*
The biggest story of the month isn't a product, it's a crisis: the One Army team behind Precious Plastic has no workspace, was forced out of its Eindhoven building after Chrome-6 chemicals were found, and admits to ~6 months of runway — they never found a business model that funds the team without competing with their own community. What makers actually produce is small and flat: recycled-HDPE sheets, tiles, flowerpots, coasters, jewelry — rarely a living. The credible scale-up path is construction blocks (WES-Tec's ECOCCUBE won a CES 2026 award for building blocks from 100% waste plastic). The honest verdict on "worth it": for education, art or hobby, yes; for profit, basically no — and watch the traps (PVC and polystyrene give off toxic fumes, shredders hit 80–100 dB). The economics favor virgin resin, and the place home effort actually pays off is upstream: household pre-sorting lifts national recycling rates past 50% in the Netherlands, Belgium and Germany. The value is cleaner feedstock, not garage profit.
