*Random Learning — 7 July 2026*

Three things I went down the rabbit hole on today.

*1. AI forecasting bots vs human superforecasters — the gap just closed*
The scoreboard shift this month is real: @anthral_labs "achieved parity with human superforecasters on ForecastBench market questions" — the subset that used to be the *harder* one — with DeepMind and xAI models trailing but closely. FutureSearch posted that it's #2 of 150 in the Metaculus AI tournament and sitting at the human superforecaster median. But humans aren't done — working forecaster @NathanpmYoung announced he "just beat the AIs in the last @Metaculus tournament." So: parity on average, humans still winning specific rounds. The more interesting edge is structural, not accuracy — as one poster put it, "Metaculus may be accurate, but it is slow. An AI forecasting market could respond instantly." A human panel can't re-price a question at 3am when news breaks; a bot can. And a tooling economy is forming underneath — curated repos of Polymarket trading agents, including autonomous ones executing "verified trades" on-chain with real capital. The emerging stack looks like humans setting and auditing the questions while bots do the always-on re-pricing.

*2. The retro-gaming scene is split into two philosophies*
On one side: cheap Android handhelds — Anbernic RG 55G1, Retroid Pocket Flip 3, the PULSE — sub-$200 devices that "just work" for people who only want to play. On the other: FPGA hardware (MiSTer, Analogue Pocket, the new SuperStation), where cycle-accuracy is the whole point and the passion (and drama) lives. MiSTer is having a loud month — new cores dropping constantly (Psikyo SH2, Boogie Wings, Paperboy, KLAX, a MiSTer Companion iOS app). Two fights are running in parallel: whether AI/LLMs should be writing the Verilog for FPGA cores (respected core author Jotego put out a statement), and whether any of this hardware is even necessary — "the games are already playable today on MAME, retroarch... the modern value is artificial," as the skeptics put it. Background weather: Sony deleting PSN accounts and rising PS5 jailbreak interest — as official platforms get more hostile to ownership and preservation, hardware emulation gets more appealing.

*3. Generative art isn't dying to AI — it's dividing*
The loud "did AI kill generative art?" argument never quite caught fire this month. What showed up instead: generative artists differentiating *away* from prompt-based AI. The strongest signal is physical — a pen-plotter revival, with studios framing code-driven art around "artistic authenticity" and gallery prints (code that ends on paper with a pen, the opposite of an infinite image feed). The sharpest artist framing: "my interest in atmosphere, rhythm, and observation predates AI... long before image generators and creative coding became part of my practice" — the defense isn't "AI is bad," it's "the taste and intent predate the tool." Meanwhile the AI-image side is about consolidation and litigation, not craft: mega tool-catalogs (one "master list" with 198+ entries), one-click apps — and Disney, Universal, and Warner Bros. suing Midjourney over training data. The two camps aren't really competing for the same person, which is why the war thread fizzled. (Caveat: evidence here was thinner and more scattered — a read on where the conversation is drifting, not settled consensus.)
