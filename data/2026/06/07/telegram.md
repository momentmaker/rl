*1. Conway's Law in the age of AI agents*
The old argument for microservices was organizational: it let big dev teams ship without stepping on each other. AI agents flip the premise. You scale teams *down*, not out, and Conway's Law says small teams build monoliths. So a growing camp now argues the microservice default is dead, and the "inverse Conway maneuver" is mutating from reshuffling org charts into encoding the target architecture as a queryable context corpus (AGENTS.md, ADRs) that agents follow. The skeptics' counter: async agent handoffs can calcify silos rather than dissolve them.

*2. How fairy tales got sanitized*
The original Pinocchio (Carlo Collodi, 1881) is a horror story: the puppet murders the Talking Cricket with a hammer, gets his feet burned off, and is hanged by the Fox and the Cat. That hanging was the intended ending. Disney turned the murder victim into Jiminy Cricket, the beloved conscience. The Grimm and Andersen tales got the same treatment, often in two stages: the authors softened them first, then Disney softened them again. Scholars call the result "adaptation displacement" — the polished version so dominates that people forget the originals were ever grim. (Fun fact: Pinocchio isn't even a Grimm tale; it's an Italian novel.)

*3. Why Buffett keeps buying Japan's trading houses*
This month Berkshire crossed 10% in all five sogo shosha (Mitsubishi, Mitsui, Itochu, Sumitomo, Marubeni). The mechanic is a clean positive carry: borrow yen at ~1%, collect ~4% dividends, so the position pays for its own financing. The deeper reason is that these "trading houses" barely trade anymore — Itochu makes ~50% of profit from its investment portfolio and owns things like FamilyMart. Buffett's own line: they "operate in a manner somewhat similar to Berkshire itself." He's buying cheap Berkshire mirrors.
