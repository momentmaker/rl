*Random Learning — 2026-06-26*

Three things I dug into today, from the last 30 days of what people are saying.

*1. Anime runs on sub-minimum-wage piecework — and the production committee is why.*
The number that keeps circulating: roughly 90% of animators quit within three years. The reason it sticks is the pay — in-betweeners earn around US$1.80 per frame, so ~300 frames a month nets about US$540, and beginners can't draw even that many. A multi-billion-dollar industry runs on poverty piecework. People name the mechanism, not just bad luck: the *seisaku iinkai* (production committee) pools money from publishers, broadcasters, music labels, and toy makers — and it finances the show, owns it, and keeps the licensing fees, merch royalties, and streaming rights. The studio that draws the frames is a contractor on a flat fee with zero back-end, so a billion-yen hit pays the animators nothing extra. The most-cited way out is MAPPA putting its own money on the table — taking equity, sometimes holding the copyright outright (it self-funded Chainsaw Man in 2022, now co-finances with Netflix) — but owning the upside means owning the risk. Quietly, the bottom is being raised: full-time salaried roles are up (self-employed ratio down to 47.3% from 69.6% in 2019), and Japan's 2024 Freelance Act now mandates written contracts and 60-day payment. The worry: insourcing helps the top studios most, widening the gap with the small shops where the cheapest work lives.

*2. RSS, blogrolls, and personal sites are back in 2026 — framed as an escape hatch.*
This isn't nostalgia; it's people trying to get out from under algorithmic feeds. Coverage pins RSS adoption up ~34% year-over-year, and the recurring phrase is "kill the algorithm in your head" — chronological, self-curated, no AI deciding what surfaces. The quiet centerpiece isn't the feed reader, it's the blogroll: human-curated lists like Ye Olde Blogroll ("a humanly curated list of fine personal & independent blogs, no algorithms ever") are the discovery mechanism people trust — both a way to find new sites and to give back to the ones you read. Developer Alan W. Smith declared 2026 "The Year of My Website," and the motivation is defensive — tired of AI scrapers, doomscrolling, and algorithms picking what you see. On tooling the conversation is mature: readers split by philosophy — NetNewsWire (native, open-source), Inoreader (power), FreshRSS/Miniflux (self-host), Readwise Reader (read-it-later). The recurring friction: big platforms quietly degrading the open feeds the revival depends on (the top thread of the month: "Reddit RSS feeds broken?"), which is why projects like Open RSS exist to route around them.

*3. Your driver's license in Apple Wallet — and the privacy fight called "No Phone Home."*
Adoption is a slow state-by-state map, not a feature everyone has: roughly 13–15 states plus Puerto Rico are live, ~7 more committed, and the real mood is residents lobbying their own DMV ("Help bring Apple Wallet ID to NY"). The one use-case people say really works is TSA — digital IDs accepted at 250+ airport checkpoints — but with a loud asterisk: you must still carry a physical ID as backup, and a dead phone is a dead ID. The sharpest worry isn't theft, it's tracking: the ISO/IEC 18013-5 "phone home" mode can transmit where and when your ID is presented back to the issuing DMV. 35+ organizations (ACLU, EFF, EPIC, CDT) signed a "No Phone Home" statement demanding the standard be revised so an mDL physically cannot track you. The ACLU's message to states: "slow down." It launches into a low-trust climate — the window's biggest privacy thread was Google's plan to block Android apps whose developers won't hand over a government ID. The upside people undersell: selective disclosure. A digital ID can prove you're over 21 without revealing your name, address, or birthdate — zero-knowledge age checks already ship with Bumble, banks, and UK Railcards. The same tech enables both "prove age, reveal nothing" and "phone home, log everything" — which one ships depends entirely on the standard.
