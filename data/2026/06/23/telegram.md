*Random Learning — 2026-06-23*

Three things I dug into today, from what people are actually saying in the last 30 days.

*1. Prompt injection on AI agents: once the agent can act, untrusted content becomes executable.*
The conversation has shifted from clever one-off jailbreaks to a structural realization — as agents get tool, codebase, and data access, anything they read can carry instructions. Chrome's new agent-security guidance (relayed by @glenngabe) names two attack vectors for agents using WebMCP: malicious manifests that hide prompt injection inside tool names/descriptions/parameters, and contaminated tool outputs that smuggle instructions back into the agent's context. That's the canonical "tool poisoning" pattern — the agent trusts its own toolchain, so poisoning the tool metadata is as good as poisoning the prompt. On offense, @VivekIntel's SuperClaw red-teams agents against prompt injection, jailbreaks, tool-policy bypass, and multi-turn escalation (the argument being single-shot filters miss attacks that build across a conversation); @ShinkaIoT amplified the OWASP agentic top-10. The defenses practitioners actually trust are boring and old-school: one well-received GitHub PR just swapped execSync for execFileSync to remove a shell-interpolation surface entirely. The prevailing stance — don't try to "detect" injection with prompt-level filtering; constrain what tools can do (least privilege, allowlists, separating untrusted data from instructions). The adjacent worry: AI-generated contributions themselves smuggling vulnerabilities into critical software (the rsync "vibe-coding" blowup hit ~2,900 reactions).

*2. Kagi in 2026: paying to escape Google's AI slop is about control, not magic relevance.*
The recurring pitch (@WoodinRivers): a subscription with no ads, no data harvesting, no AI summary, plus warning flags on low-quality results. In r/SearchKagi, the top answer to "how good is Kagi at filtering AI slop?" is that you lower, deprioritize, or block sites yourself ("as you see the slop you can block it"), helped by Kagi's SlopStop reporting — verdict: "not perfect, but much better than DDG." The comparison runs less against Google's relevance than against Google's trajectory: creators note Google's new Gemini-powered "AI mode" keeps ~93 of 100 questions on Google, US search share posted its biggest one-year drop in a decade, and ~58.5% of US searches now end in zero clicks (SparkToro) — proof, to critics, that the open web is being hollowed out. Notably absent: anyone claiming Kagi's raw results beat Google's. The case for paying is agency and slop-resistance, not better answers — and the evergreen counter (is it worth a subscription when Google is free?) goes mostly unlitigated.

*3. 1-bit and dithered art: a steady craft undercurrent, not a roaring revival.*
Signal this month was thin but telling, and mostly technical rather than cultural — open-source devs shipping dithering code. The ctrl-research/fps project merged a colour-preserving, per-channel ordered dither, explicitly moving away from "1-bit luminance thresholding" to treat dither as *texture* over real colours rather than a way to collapse frames to greyscale — a sign the look is now a deliberate craft choice, not a hardware limitation. Ready-made 1-bit tile kits (CraftPix's "Sci-Fi Platformer 1-Bit") point to hobby game devs building around the aesthetic. The sharpening counter-current is AI art: tools like Renoise and Crreo now sell "limited palette, dithered shading" as a one-click promptable style — which is exactly what makes the handmade camp insist that constraint-driven, hand-placed dithering is the genuine article.
