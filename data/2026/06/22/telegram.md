*Random Learning — 2026-06-22*

Three things I dug into today, from what people are actually saying in the last 30 days.

*1. AI is hollowing out how-to and self-help books — and authors are saying it out loud.*
Tim Ferriss's June essay lit the fuse: his prescriptive-nonfiction sales are down ~57% year-over-year and approaching ~80% off their 2022 peak, and he blames AI directly — "any constellation of footnotes" can't explain a near-vertical drop. The dominant author thesis is brutal and simple: a how-to book is a lookup table and a decision tree, and ChatGPT gives you both, free. The trade press is anxious rather than dismissive (Jane Friedman, Northeastern: adult nonfiction down 9%, self-help down 26% YoY). Pushback says blame is multi-causal — Libby/free library reading, the internet, podcasts — and that the survivable niche is lived personal narrative an AI can't fake ("here's how I wrecked my marriage and the 3 lessons"). The cruel twist: the category is also being buried from the supply side — one study found 77% of Amazon "Success" self-help titles were likely AI-written. Thin demand AND a synthetic slush pile.

*2. Link rot is worse than the vibes — and citations are the scariest casualty.*
About 38% of web pages that existed in 2013 are already gone (Pew); Ahrefs puts rot at 66.5% for links sampled since 2013. Decay is exponential with age — scholarly citations fall from ~87% reachable at 0–5 years to 38% past a decade. The "digital dark age" stops being a slogan in the courts: roughly half of the URLs in U.S. Supreme Court opinions are dead, and 70%+ of Harvard Law Review URLs have rotted — meaning a cited source may soon "be known solely from those citations." The 2026 shift is from save-it-yourself to fix-it-automatically: Automattic + Internet Archive shipped a WordPress plugin that silently redirects dead links to the Wayback copy, and Wikipedia's IABOT already archives pre-emptively at scale. The grassroots anxiety is cost, not apathy (r/DataHoarder: "the prices are killing data hoarding"); the practitioner answer is self-hosting with ArchiveBox plus Perma.cc permalinks — own the bytes, trust no single point of failure.

*3. Full-body AI health scanners (Neko, Prenuvo): doctors hate them, buyers love the vibe.*
The cleanest framing of the month: "investors love them, doctors hate them." Elective whole-body MRI has no evidence of mortality or quality-of-life benefit and may cause harm through overdiagnosis. The numbers explain the clinical nervousness — 20–40% of scans throw incidental findings, one review found 95% of participants had *some* abnormal finding but under 0.5% were suspicious for cancer, and in a 3,371-person study 62% of resulting biopsies showed no malignancy. No major society endorses it for average-risk asymptomatic adults; there's even a lawsuit alleging Prenuvo missed a stroke (a clean scan breeds dangerous false reassurance). Yet people who paid mostly liked the experience — futuristic, reassuring — which is a different question from clinical value. Neko (Spotify's Daniel Ek) sells "affordable luxury" longevity at ~£299, but reviewers split between "great peace of mind" and "weak offering for the price." The honest verdict: more defensible for genuine family-history/genetic risk than for the worried well.
