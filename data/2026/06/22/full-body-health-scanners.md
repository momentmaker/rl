What I learned:

**The doctors-vs-investors split is the whole story** - The sharpest framing in the last 30 days came from [The Hindu](https://www.thehindu.com/sci-tech/science/whole-body-scans-why-investors-love-them-but-doctors-hate-them/article71015200.ece): "investors love them but doctors hate them." Elective whole-body MRI "lacks evidence of mortality or quality-of-life benefit and may cause harm through overdiagnosis, unnecessary procedures, and psychological distress." On X, a viral thread captured the clinical pushback bluntly: "Doctors are dunking on the full-body scanner... it's not hard to generate 'abnormal' findings (benign lesions and tumors) → follow-up imaging → specialist visits → biopsies → potentially surgery," warning that at population scale, overdiagnosis "can lead to more deaths and complications than lives saved."

**The incidental-findings numbers are why clinicians are nervous** - This is the most-cited hard data across sources. [Biograph](https://www.biograph.com/blog/whole-body-mri-early-detection) and others note systematic reviews showing 20-40% of scans produce incidental findings of unclear significance; one review found "95% of participants had abnormal findings, about a third required further investigation, and fewer than 0.5% had findings suspicious for cancer." A 3,371-person study via [Health Review Network](https://healthreviewnetwork.com/wellbeing/prenuvo-mri-screening-review/) found 30.3% got an incidental finding and 62.1% of resulting biopsies showed no malignancy. [Michigan Medicine](https://www.michiganmedicine.org/health-lab/whole-body-mris-arent-beneficial-they-seem) and the American College of Radiology both decline to recommend total-body MRI for average-risk, asymptomatic adults - unlike mammography, colonoscopy, and low-dose CT, it has no USPSTF backing.

**The "false reassurance" risk got a real-world cautionary tale** - [The Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/health/2026/01/13/prenuvo-lawsuit-full-body-scan/) reported a lawsuit alleging Prenuvo's full-body MRI missed signs of a catastrophic stroke. This is the flip side critics keep raising: scans miss things too (the X thread flagged "false negatives... early colon" cancers), so a clean scan can breed dangerous overconfidence.

**People who actually tried it mostly liked the experience - and that's separate from whether it's medically useful** - First-person testimony skews positive on vibe and reassurance. A TikTok reviewer via [Full Body Health Scan](https://www.tiktok.com/discover/full-body-health-scan) said of Prenuvo: "Overall I'm so happy I finally have answers about my health. Totally worth it." UK reviewers of Neko, like the [Cotswold Journal](https://www.cotswoldjournal.co.uk/news/26135301.full-body-scan-neko-health-results-shocked/), called it "a great way to reassure yourself that you have good health, but also it's so important for identifying problems before they become problems." The recurring user verdict: the experience is futuristic and reassuring, but that's a different question from clinical value.

**Neko's positioning is "affordable luxury," and the value complaints are loudest there** - [Vogue](https://www.vogue.com/article/neko-wants-to-be-the-affordable-luxury-of-longevity) frames Neko (from Spotify's Daniel Ek) as the faster, cheaper, more scalable answer to Prenuvo and Ezra's $2,500+ MRIs - roughly £299/$400 for an hour-long AI check-up. But [Trustpilot reviewers](https://www.trustpilot.com/review/nekohealth.com) are split: praise for efficient, knowledgeable staff and free follow-ups, against complaints it's "a very weak offering for the price" delivering "simple details already available through a GP."

**Celebrity marketing is fueling the hype the doctors are pushing back on** - [@ParisHilton](https://x.com/ParisHilton/status/2061901999931613529) plugged Prenuvo to her followers ("Love @prenuvo for its whole body MRI, blood biomarker testing and personalized results") with a free-scan giveaway, and Prenuvo is bundled into perks like Chase Sapphire Reserve and Hims memberships. That influencer-and-perks engine is exactly the "uptake has moved faster than the evidence base" dynamic clinicians flag.

KEY PATTERNS from the research:
1. The expert consensus is "not for healthy asymptomatic people" - no major cancer or radiology society endorses whole-body MRI screening absent risk factors, per [Michigan Medicine](https://www.michiganmedicine.org/health-lab/whole-body-mris-arent-beneficial-they-seem)
2. Incidental findings are the core harm: high abnormal-finding rates, low confirmed-cancer yield, cascading biopsies that are mostly benign, per [Biograph](https://www.biograph.com/blog/whole-body-mri-early-detection)
3. People who paid mostly liked the experience and the reassurance, even when the medical payoff is unproven, per first-person TikTok and local-press reviews
4. "Worth it" is conditional - skeptics and even proponents converge on: more defensible for those with strong family history or genetic risk than for the average healthy adult, per [The Hindu](https://www.thehindu.com/sci-tech/science/whole-body-scans-why-investors-love-them-but-doctors-hate-them/article71015200.ece)
5. False negatives matter too - a clean scan can create dangerous false reassurance, dramatized by the Prenuvo missed-stroke lawsuit, per [The Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/health/2026/01/13/prenuvo-lawsuit-full-body-scan/)
