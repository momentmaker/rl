*Random Learning — 2026-06-14*

Three things I went down the rabbit hole on today.

*1. AI data centers and who actually pays for the grid*
The framing flipped this year from "AI is cool" to "who pays the bill." The biggest thread of the month (31k upvotes) was a bill requiring data centers to pay for their own power, and states are moving first: Tennessee now forces 50MW+ centers to fund their own substations and transmission, with 300+ data-center bills filed across 30+ states and 36+ utilities adopting large-load tariffs. The hard number everyone cites is PJM - the grid operator for 67M people across 13 states - where data centers drove a 76% jump in wholesale power costs in Q1 2026, fueling actual talk of breaking the operator up. Hyperscalers are countering with "we'll pay our own way" pledges (Anthropic promised 100% of grid-upgrade costs; Microsoft and OpenAI made similar ones), but people read them as PR until the cost-socializing loopholes close. Sleeper theme: resentment over local tax breaks for projects seen as net-negative on jobs and bills.

*2. Why Japan keeps killing its rural train lines*
Over 30 years Japan has discontinued 1,366 km of track - about 5% of the network - and the pace is accelerating, with roughly a third of the loss in Hokkaido. The 115-year-old Rumoi Main Line ran its last train on March 31, 2026, and it died the way they all do now: in slices, segment by segment over a decade. The math is brutal and the privatized JR companies are done absorbing it - they're now in formal "keep it or kill it" talks over 21 loss-making sections, with 122 lines carrying under 1,000 riders a day on the de facto death-row list. The favored fix, converting lines to bus rapid transit, is already fraying: the same depopulation that emptied the trains means there aren't enough bus drivers either, and 20.7% of the population already lives in a "transportation desert." The recurring contradiction: crowds show up for the last-train funeral, but not for the daily 7am train.

*3. What actually makes a dataviz project go viral and hit the HN front page*
Show HN posts reach the front page under 2% of the time, and the people who beat the odds insist it's craft, not luck. The real ranking signal is intellectual curiosity, which favors weird specificity over polish - a hand-built "map of people who lived in the Roman Empire" beats a slick dashboard cold. On Reddit, the runaway hits hand the audience an argument ("Trump's Iran deal has been imminent for 11 weeks," 7.5k upvotes) or a shocking clean delta ("bots are now half of web traffic, up from 30%"). The shareable unit is the before/after jump, not the chart type. And speed is non-negotiable: the front page is a traffic weapon aimed at your own server, so a heavy WebGL warm-up or a 180MB payload dies before the discussion even forms. Title factually, ship a try-it-now link, credit your data.
