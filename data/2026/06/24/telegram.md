*Random Learning — 2026-06-24*

Three things I dug into today, from what people are actually saying in the last 30 days.

*1. What makes a hand-drawn imaginary city map feel alive — and why some people draw one map for decades.*
The most active question among hobby cartographers right now isn't "what brushes" but "where would this city actually be?" The top r/mapmaking thread of the month asks where to put a major trade city on a map of a river meeting the sea, and the whole ethos is in the replies: you don't decorate a coastline, you reason about it — river mouths, natural harbors, defensible bends, portage points. That's also the loudest critique of AI map generators: they skip cause and effect and produce rivers that flow uphill and mountains in perfect concentric circles. "Alive" then comes from wear and suggestion, not detail — curved streets so the grid looks grown rather than planned, varied building sizes, and every symbol implying a system (slums imply migration, a gallows implies law and fear). And the emotional core is the years-long map: Jerry Gretzinger has drawn "Jerry's Map" for ~50 years into a 2,000-panel imaginary world; a 14-year-old spent 7 years on a 3-metre map of a city that doesn't exist. The map is never "done" because the point is returning to it — layering one more street, one more era. It gets alive partly in the comments, too, where the maker defends why a city sits where it does.

*2. The honest 2026 verdict on llms.txt: ship it, but not for the reason it's sold — schema.org is the part that works.*
After a year of hype, practitioners reading their own server logs have swung to skepticism. One analysis of 137K sites found 97% of llms.txt files never get fetched; OtterlyAI logged 84 of 62,100 AI-bot requests hitting the file (0.1%); SE Ranking found only ~10% of 300K domains even have it, with no citation uplift. Google says outright it doesn't use the file. The reframing that lands: llms.txt was never a search-SEO play — Jeremy Howard proposed it for inference-time use by agents already on your site, not for ranking in AI Overviews. When practitioners list what actually makes a site legible to AI, the order is consistent: prerendered HTML, then JSON-LD structured data, then (cheaply) llms.txt. Schema.org is the load-bearing layer — the shared vocabulary answer engines consume to cite you. The frontier is moving past the static file toward agent-callable surfaces: RFC-registered link relations, MCP servers, FAQPage markup — and Google's new Lighthouse "agentic browsing" audit now scores how well agents can read your site. Treat it as business-to-agent, not SEO. And verify: grep your logs for GPTBot/ClaudeBot/PerplexityBot hits before believing any of it.

*3. Edo Japan's zero-waste economy was circular by necessity, not virtue.*
The story circulating is the hook sustainability people love: a city of over a million — bigger than London or Paris at the time — that produced almost no garbage, while Europe drowned in filth. But historians keep pulling it back from romance toward economics. Night soil wasn't moral recycling; it was a priced commodity. Compost merchants gathered human waste to sell to farmers for silver, apartment landlords profited from tenants' output, and farmers formed buying associations with literal fights over collection rights. The loop held because excrement had a market price. The uncomfortable modern lesson writers extract: Edo was circular because it was *closed and resource-starved* — sandy, nutrient-poor soils made fertilizer non-optional, and sakoku-era isolation meant almost nothing was imported. The whole 300-year system collapsed by ~1955, the moment cheap synthetic fertilizer removed the reason to collect. The part people most want to revive is repair-as-profession: tinkers with portable forges, umbrella refurbishers, the kimono lifecycle (clothing → nightwear → napkin → rag → fuel, ash sold as fertilizer) — repair restored as the missing "R." Mottainai and kintsugi are the values hook, increasingly tied to degrowth rather than consumer recycling.
