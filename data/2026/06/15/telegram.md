*Random Learning — 2026-06-15*

Three things I went down the rabbit hole on today.

*1. Running a company in Japan as a foreigner just got much harder*
Japan's October 16, 2025 reform redrew the whole map for the Business Manager visa. To qualify now you need ¥30 million in paid-in capital (up roughly six-fold from the old ¥5 million working floor), at least one full-time Japanese or permanent-resident employee, three years of management experience, and JLPT N2-level Japanese. A key clarification founders keep making: that capital is not a fee - it is money you deposit and leave parked inside the company, so you budget for locked-up cash, not just filing costs. The kabushiki kaisha (KK) also carries a real annual compliance burden (tax filings, social insurance, bookkeeping), which is why many people now weigh the lighter godo kaisha (GK) instead - it can still support the visa and a corporate bank account. The mood among foreign owners is anxious: family-run restaurants are squeezed hardest, and the reform meant to "weed out paper companies" is catching legitimate businesses too.

*2. Almost nobody who tried the four-day week went back*
The most-cited number right now: over 90% of companies in the UK four-day-week trial kept it, and 92% stuck with it after the six-month pilot. In the 4 Day Week Global cohort (61 companies, ~3,000 workers), 18 firms made it permanent outright and 38 more continued. The reason executives did not revert is financial - revenue held or rose about 1.4% over the trial. The real ROI argument is people: burnout fell 71% and turnover fell 57%, so you replace fewer staff. Juliet Schor's framing is that productivity is concentrated, and a full day off beats scattered slack time - you are buying less waste, not less work. US adoption is creeping up (22% of employers offer some version, up from 14% in 2022) but it is still a minority practice anchored in the firms that ran structured trials. Skeptics remain, but 15 trial workers said no amount of money would get them back to five days.

*3. What actually stops keyless car theft*
The two attacks owners defend against are mechanically different, and that decides what works. A relay attack uses paired signal boosters - one near your fob through a house wall, one near the car - to unlock and start in under 30 seconds with no hacking. CAN injection is different: thieves physically pry open a headlight or wiring junction, splice onto the car's CAN bus, and inject fake "valid key" messages that bypass the fob entirely. That is why a Faraday pouch is necessary but not sufficient - it blocks the relay attack but does nothing against a thief cutting into your loom. Keyless techniques now drive ~60% of US vehicle thefts (over 850,000 cars in 2024, one every 37 seconds). The defenses owners actually trust are the visible physical ones - steering locks and OBD port locks - because thieves optimize for a fast, quiet grab; the goal is to be the harder car on the street. The deeper fix (motion-sensor fobs, PIN-to-drive, CAN intrusion detection) lives in the car itself, but sealing vehicle networks collides with right-to-repair.
