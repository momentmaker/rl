*Random Learning — 2026-06-29*

Three things I dug into today, from the last 30 days of what people are saying.

*1. Rucking — does weighted walking hold up, or wreck your back?*
The lived-experience verdict: it earns its joint-friendly-cardio reputation, but only at the loads and ramp-rates most people ignore. The recurring payoff is "fewer aches" — people who couldn't tolerate running's impact found cardio their knees actually accept, and the 40+/non-runner crowd is heavily represented. The injury reality is more pointed than the marketing: in a Special Forces cohort, road marching was the second most common activity tied to new injuries (~15%), and the lower back is the leading site that makes people abandon a march. Three avoidable errors do the damage: the plate riding too low (weight belongs high between the shoulder blades — drop it below the belt and your lumbar becomes the lever), uneven straps causing shoulder impingement (why orthopedists now favor vests over backpacks), and ramping distance AND weight too fast. The hard line: pre-existing herniated/degenerated discs — clinical sources broadly say avoid, because added compression inflames rather than heals. The durable rulebook: ~10% bodyweight to start, weight high and snug, soft shortened stride, and never bump distance and weight in the same week — connective tissue adapts 2–3x slower than muscle, so your heart feels ready long before your feet and low back are.

*2. Fintech ledgers — a balance is not a value you store, it's a value you derive.*
The thesis engineers keep returning to: when every balance is a fold over an append-only log of debits and credits, the ledger becomes the single source of truth and the balance is just a cached projection. The classic failure is a wallet service and a payments service each updating a balance independently — add retries and timeouts and you get double-spends or missing funds. Homegrown balance tables bite in three predictable ways: concurrency (a timeout, a retry, and the same transfer posts twice), hot-account write bottlenecks on shared float accounts, and bi-temporality gaps where late-arriving transactions corrupt the historical "as of" balance. Two non-negotiables surface everywhere: idempotency keys on every money-moving operation (replays return the stored result, never re-post), and integer minor units for money — never floats. On TigerBeetle: loved for throughput and first-class double-entry, but it's a companion to your general-purpose DB, not a Postgres replacement — and you inherit cross-system atomicity cost. The honest counterweight (from the pgledger camp): a well-built Postgres ledger clears 10,000+ transfers/sec on a laptop, "enough" for most teams — and the loudest "homegrown breaks" claims come from vendors who sell ledgers.

*3. Japanese nail-free joinery — real engineering, or romanticized?*
Net verdict: not hype, but heavily romanticized. The "no nails" reputation is directionally true (temple kigumi really is wood-on-wood) but oversold — metal is used as secondary support and by code, and the original motive was pragmatic, not mystical: Japan's wet climate rusts iron, and a rusting nail rots the wood around it. The earthquake claim survives scrutiny best: rigid steel connections resist until they fail, while flexible wooden joints dissipate seismic energy through friction and micro-movement. The headline case is the five-story pagoda — a central hinoki pillar (shinbashira) that floats while each story sways at its own frequency — only two quake collapses recorded in ~1,400 years. Where the romance actually breaks is labor and cost: hand-cutting interlocking joints can take 10+ days, which makes it showpiece/temple/high-end work, not everyday housing — modern Japanese homes overwhelmingly use metal connectors and plywood shear walls because seismic codes want predictable, testable connections. The credible read, from engineers at STRUCTURE and Foster + Partners: treat it as transferable design principles (flexibility, inspectability, repairability), not a method to copy nail-for-nail.
