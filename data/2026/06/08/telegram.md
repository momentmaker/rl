*Random Learning — 2026-06-08*

Three things I went down the rabbit hole on today.

*1. The 1,200-year Kyoto cherry blossom record*
Japanese courtiers have logged the date Kyoto's cherry blossoms hit full bloom since 812 AD, which makes it the longest phenological record on Earth. For roughly a thousand years the date sat around mid-April with ordinary weather noise. Since the 1950s it has lurched about 17 days earlier, and 2026's peak fell on March 29. A 2022 Met Office attribution study pinned about 11 of those days on human-caused warming and found the record-early 2021 bloom roughly 40x more likely because of it. The honest debate isn't "is it warming" but "how much is global CO2 vs Kyoto's own urban heat island" - and the city's heat effect looks to have plateaued around 2000 while the blooms kept advancing. The late Yasuyuki Aono built the dataset by hand from court diaries and temple logs; after his 2025 death a new custodian stepped in to keep the 1,200-year chain unbroken.

*2. The hippie trail and its modern revival*
From the mid-1950s to 1979, young Westerners traveled overland about 6,000 miles from London to India and Nepal: London to Istanbul to Tehran to Kabul, over the Khyber Pass, down to Delhi and Kathmandu. It ran on its own analog social network - the Pudding Shop in Istanbul, Kabul's Chicken Street, Kathmandu's Freak Street - plus cheap bus lines (Indiaman, Magic Bus) that turned it into a product. Tony and Maureen Wheeler's 1972 drive produced the first Lonely Planet guide. Then two shocks slammed it shut in a single year: the 1979 Iranian Revolution and the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan. The modern revival, Adventures Overland's "Bus to London," honors the dream but reroutes east through Myanmar, China, and Central Asia to avoid the still-impassable Iran-Afghanistan heart. At around 15 lakh rupees it's a luxury expedition now, not the pennies-a-day pilgrimage it once was.

*3. Pigments once worth more than gold*
Natural ultramarine came from lapis lazuli mined in Afghanistan, hauled to Venice (oltremarino = "beyond the sea"), then refined over weeks, so it cost more than gold and masters rationed it for the Virgin's robe. Tyrian purple was worse: on the order of 10,000 murex sea snails per gram, which is why purple meant emperors (a modern Tunisian dyer still crushes about 45kg of snails to get a single gram worth ~$2,700). Indian yellow's bizarre cow-urine origin story was dismissed as folklore until a 2018 chemistry study found hippuric acid in the original samples. And plenty of gorgeous colors were quietly lethal: arsenic-based Scheele's green, mercury vermilion, lead white. The deepest lesson is restraint - the four-pigment Zorn palette shows that a limited set forces harmony, the same reason a constrained brand palette reads as intentional instead of noisy.
