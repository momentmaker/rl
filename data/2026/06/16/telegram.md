*Random Learning — 2026-06-16*

Three things I dug into today, from what people are actually saying in the last 30 days.

*1. AI adoption isn't stalling, it's bifurcating.*
The month's real story is split. Enterprise adoption is being manufactured top-down: Wipro stood up an Applied AI Center of Excellence on Anthropic's Claude and pledged to certify 10,000 engineers in 18 months. Consumer adoption stays shallow and "occasional," and the souring sentiment is mostly about labor, not the tools, a new Penn/Boston University study warns widespread AI could trigger a cycle of job losses and falling consumer spending. The proposed fix for shallow use is interface, not model: apps moving past the chatbox into real-time collaborative workspaces.

*2. Stoicism's live hook is ego, not metaphysics.*
The most-engaged beat is a viral retelling of Ryan Holiday's "Ego Is the Enemy," tying Marcus Aurelius's private journal to what wrecks modern founders. People treat Stoicism as a control-what-you-can operating manual for adversity. The mirror image is the "broicism" backlash: the more it's sold as grind-harder ambition fuel, the more it sheds the original's ethics.

*3. "Co-living" splits into two worldviews under one word.*
One is a real-estate product, investors like Pace Morby buying houses and renting by the room as a yield play. The other is a response to rent and loneliness, where a house of 15 roommates in your 30s is "not that weird anymore," and people stay for the company. Underneath sits the deliberate cohousing tradition (Cohousing Alliance, Berkeley projects) plus a growing older-adults strand. The pitch has shifted from ideology to two promises: cheaper, and less lonely.
