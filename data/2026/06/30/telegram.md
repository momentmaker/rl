*Random Learning — 2026-06-30*

Three things I dug into today, from the last 30 days of what people are saying.

*1. "Default alive" vs raising VC — what holds up once a tiny team tries it?*
The lived verdict: AI genuinely makes "default alive" cheap to reach, but it's a profitability tool, not a dominance tool. Solo founders report collapsing their cost base to almost nothing — one put it as "AI didn't turn me into a 10x dev, it just let me run a whole company by myself," and the share of no-VC solo-founder startups has climbed from 22% (2015) to 38% (2024). The pro-bootstrap case is unit economics, not vibes: ~77% of solopreneurs profitable inside 12 months, ~$50/mo of API spend replacing a Series A team. The pro-VC counterpunch is the sharpest reframe of the month: "AI compresses the time it takes to do things, but not the time it takes for things to happen." If you can ship an MVP in four weeks, so can every competitor — speed stopped being rare, so it stopped being a moat. What still takes years (network density, regulation, proprietary data, enterprise trust) is where a check still makes sense. The silent killer is cash flow plus linearly-scaling AI costs; 82% of AI-startup failures trace to cash-flow mismanagement. The 2026 consensus is sequencing, not a binary: bootstrap to profitability, keep the equity, raise only for a moat that genuinely needs years and capital.

*2. Why Texans treat H-E-B as more than a grocery store.*
The phrase that stuck this month is "FEMA of Texas." What people point to isn't charity — it's a standing emergency-ops operation run 365 days a year: a 15+ vehicle convoy of Disaster Relief Units, two 45-foot Mobile Kitchens plating up to 6,000 meals/day each, water and fuel tankers, plus DRUs that roll with a working pharmacy, ATM and check-cashing so displaced residents can fill prescriptions and pay bills. Locals report the relief trucks arrived before government aid and were the last to leave. The second pillar of the loyalty is structural: H-E-B is privately held — the largest private employer in the state — and shares the upside through a Partner Stock Plan that granted stock to ~55,000 employees, who are expected to own ~15% of the company over time. The argument fans make is that not answering to public shareholders is exactly what lets H-E-B fund a standing disaster fleet that would never survive a quarterly-earnings call. On Reddit the devotion shows up as shorthand — the top thread is a near-wordless one literally titled "Why…". (One sour note: a June 20 in-store confrontation went viral into a doxxing saga — the stores are also where culture-war flashpoints play out.)

*3. Proof-of-personhood — what people make of "prove you're human" online.*
It stopped being theoretical the moment Reddit floated making bot-flagged accounts verify with Face ID, passkeys, or government ID. The reaction was mostly hostile: people balk at biometrics to post in "a meme subreddit," and anonymity is the dealbreaker — even Alexis Ohanian admitted "I just don't know how to sell face-scanning to redditors." The mood underneath is exhaustion, not enthusiasm for ID — the window's biggest threads are about AI backlash and slop fatigue, which makes people wary of the "solution" too, because the entities selling personhood gates are the Big-Tech/crypto players they already distrust. World ID (Sam Altman's iris-scanning Orb) owns the airtime and the most baggage: boosters pitch "anonymous proof of human" via zero-knowledge proofs; skeptics see "cataloguing eyeballs" and point to GDPR bans across several countries. Developers add a technical correction: passkeys prove device possession plus a human gesture, not a *unique* human — one person can mint unlimited passkeys, so they stop scripts, not bot-farms. Signed content (C2PA) certifies history, not honesty — researchers already forged a valid manifest attributing an AI image to a real camera. The intellectually respected path isn't a universal biometric gate but Vitalik Buterin's "pluralistic identity" — many overlapping issuers so no single one wins outright.
