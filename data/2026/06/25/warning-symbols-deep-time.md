The engine pulled 51 items but the high-scoring ones were off-topic noise (a WOW-signal video, a Taiwan-2027 prophecy, Calgary's noise bylaw) that the ranker flagged as entity-misses. The actual conversation lives in evergreen design writing, Wikipedia, and government nuclear-waste agencies - and it clusters hard around one trigger: Finland's [Onkalo repository](https://www.euronews.com/2026/04/09/in-finland-the-worlds-first-facility-to-bury-nuclear-waste-is-set-to-begin-operations) starting operations in 2026, which reopened the oldest argument in the field.

**The "leave no marker" turn is the live debate** - The most current and most counterintuitive position is Finland's: when Onkalo fills, the plan is to seal it, replant the forest, and leave nothing - no obelisk, no warning, no sign. The operator's reasoning, per [Science / AAAS](https://www.science.org/content/article/finland-built-tomb-store-nuclear-waste-can-it-survive-100000-years) and [Indian Defence Review](https://indiandefencereview.com/finland-onkalo-nuclear-waste-repository-no-markers/), is that across a 100,000-year gap any monument that says "do not dig here" reads instead as "something valuable is buried here." A warning, given enough time, becomes a treasure map. This directly inverts the older US design-it-bigger-and-scarier instinct.

**The canonical design answer is layered, language-free menace** - The reference point everyone still argues with is the [1992-93 Sandia National Laboratories report](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long-term_nuclear_waste_warning_messages) for the WIPP site. Its move was to NOT rely on language: build foreboding physical earthworks (spike fields, menacing blocks, jagged "landscape of thorns") that read as man-made and hostile on sight, then add pictographs, then add written text in many languages as the innermost layer for whoever can still read. The governing line - "this place is not a place of honor... no highly esteemed deed is commemorated here" - became a [late-2010s copypasta](https://www.damninteresting.com/this-place-is-not-a-place-of-honor/), which is now the most-shared piece of this whole field on the social web.

**The weird proposals are doing serious semiotic work** - Bastide and Fabbri's "[ray cats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_cat)" (cats genetically tuned to change color near radiation, with folk songs and myths bred to carry the meaning across generations) and the "atomic priesthood" (an institution that ritually re-transmits the danger) aren't jokes - they're attempts to solve the part the obelisks can't: keeping meaning alive when the symbols themselves rot. Germany's nuclear-safety agency [BASE](https://www.base.bund.de/en/repository/preservation-of-knowledge/faq/atomic-semiotics-nuclear-priests-radiation-cats.html) catalogs them seriously as historical thought experiments, and a Montreal lab revived the idea as the [Ray Cat Solution](http://www.theraycatsolution.com/) movement in 2015.

**The Long Now framing is "redundancy and institutions, not monuments"** - The Long Now Foundation's contribution is less a marker design than a stance: meaning survives deep time through redundant, multi-format archives and living institutions, not single objects. Its [Rosetta Project](https://www.nationalgeographic.com/premium/article/nuclear-waste-repository-ray-cat-wipp-hitf-fabbri) - the Universal Declaration of Human Rights micro-etched in 600+ languages on a durable disc, explicitly modeled on the Rosetta Stone - is the constructive version of the same problem: don't bet everything on one carrier surviving and one future reader decoding it.

KEY PATTERNS from the research:
1. The core design principle is "communicate menace without language" - physical hostility first (earthworks/spikes), pictographs second, text last, per the [Sandia/WIPP layered model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long-term_nuclear_waste_warning_messages).
2. The 2026 frontier is the reversal: Finland's bet that the safest marker is no marker, because warnings invite excavation, per [Science/AAAS](https://www.science.org/content/article/finland-built-tomb-store-nuclear-waste-can-it-survive-100000-years).
3. "Awe, pride, admiration" is the survival heuristic - Sandia argued only emotionally resonant sites get remembered, which is also why "this is not a place of honor" deliberately strips honor away, per [Damn Interesting](https://www.damninteresting.com/this-place-is-not-a-place-of-honor/).
4. The genuinely hard sub-problem is meaning-decay, not material-decay - obelisks outlast their own legibility, which is why proposals like [ray cats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_cat) and atomic priesthoods try to encode the warning in biology, myth, and ritual instead of stone.
5. The Long Now / Rosetta answer is redundancy across formats and languages plus living institutions, betting on many fragile carriers over one durable monument, per [BASE's knowledge-preservation framing](https://www.base.bund.de/en/repository/preservation-of-knowledge/atomic-semiotics/atomic-semiotics.html).
