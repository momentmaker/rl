*Random Learning — 2026-06-25*

Three things I dug into today, from what people are actually saying in the last 30 days.

*1. Apple silicon Mac mini and Studio became the local-LLM box — and they keep selling out.*
The loudest signal of the month is scarcity, and the local-AI crowd reads it as vindication: "Every Mac Studio is now GONE except for the 96gb one." But the real cause isn't an Apple stumble — it's a DRAM shortage. AI-server memory demand drove roughly a 90% price surge, so Apple cut high-RAM configs in May, killed the $599 mini, and left the M3 Ultra Studio at 96GB only, with Tim Cook warning the squeeze "may take several months." Why people buy these for inference: unified memory. The pitch is capacity, not speed — "what fits" beats "how fast." A $1,799 Mac runs 33B models that fit on no consumer NVIDIA GPU. The honest tradeoff is acknowledged: Apple is slower per token (an RTX 4090 beats an M4 Max on 7B because inference is memory-bandwidth bound) and falls off a cliff under concurrent load. Where it wins is the quiet, always-on, single-box desktop — a 70B model runs at a usable 8-9 tok/s in unified memory while a split VRAM/RAM PC crawls at ~2, and it pulls 25-35W vs ~450W for a 4090 rig. The settled consensus: MLX is the software stack, M5 Pro 64GB Mac mini is the value pick, and an M5 refresh this summer is pulling demand forward.

*2. How do you warn people 10,000 years from now? Finland's answer is to leave no marker at all.*
Finland's Onkalo repository starting operations in 2026 reopened the oldest argument in nuclear semiotics, and its position is the counterintuitive one: when the vault fills, seal it, replant the forest, and leave nothing — no obelisk, no sign. The reasoning is that across a 100,000-year gap, any monument saying "do not dig here" eventually reads as "something valuable is buried here." A warning becomes a treasure map. That inverts the canonical 1992 Sandia design for the US WIPP site, which tried to communicate menace *without* language: hostile earthworks and spike fields first ("landscape of thorns"), pictographs second, text last — the line "this place is not a place of honor... no highly esteemed deed is commemorated here" (now a beloved internet copypasta). The genuinely hard part is meaning-decay, not material-decay: obelisks outlast their own legibility. That's why the weird proposals — "ray cats" bred to change color near radiation, with folk songs to carry the meaning; an "atomic priesthood" that ritually re-transmits the danger — are doing serious work. The Long Now framing is the constructive version: bet on redundancy across formats and languages and living institutions, not one durable monument.

*3. Nintendo DMCA'd a dozen emulators at once — and the web-based ones can't be deleted.*
After the 2024 Yuzu settlement ($2.4M) killed the project, the community assumed forks would carry on. In February 2026 Nintendo fired DMCA notices at ~12 Switch emulators and forks on GitHub in one sweep (Citron, Eden, Ryujinx, Sudachi, Suyu, and more). The legal theory isn't that emulator code infringes — it's that it circumvents Nintendo's encryption under DMCA §1201, so cleanly reverse-engineered code is "likely legal" yet still gets pulled. The community line: emulation is legal, downloading ROMs you don't own isn't, and keys/encryption are the real chokepoint. Preservation policy split hard in June 2026: the EU declined the Stop Killing Games campaign (~1.3M signatures) while California's Assembly passed a bill requiring offline modes or refunds before killing online games. The structural shift underneath all this: browser/WebAssembly emulators (EmulatorJS, the Internet Archive's WASM platforms) turn the emulator into an un-installable webpage — there's no binary to take down. The pattern is settlement → shutdown → fork → takedown → re-fork, and it doesn't stop: Eden reached Google Play in January 2026 even as its siblings were being DMCA'd.
