What I learned:

**Cities are canceling Flock contracts faster than ever, and ICE is the trigger** - The biggest story of the window is the accelerating municipal revolt. At least 50 cities have now canceled Flock contracts, deactivated cameras, or rejected proposals, with most of the action in the last three months, per [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/02/17/nx-s1-5612825/flock-contracts-canceled-immigration-survillance-concerns) - Flagstaff, Cambridge, Eugene, and Santa Cruz among them. The through-line is fear that local plate data feeds a federal deportation dragnet. In [Framingham, MA](https://www.metrowestdailynews.com/story/news/local/2026/06/02/framingham-ma-group-wants-police-to-drop-license-plate-reader-program-flock-safety/90294622007/) this month, a residents' group pressed police to drop the program, with one organizer warning that "even if ICE doesn't have access" directly, there's no way to know why another department is pulling Framingham's data.

**A wrongful month-in-jail case became the viral skeptic's exhibit A** - The single hottest discussion thread was the [Times of San Diego](https://timesofsandiego.com/crime/2026/06/07/a-flock-license-plate-reader-linked-a-san-diego-man-to-a-violent-crime-he-was-five-miles-away/) story (88 points, 47 comments on [Hacker News](https://news.ycombinator.com/)) about a man who spent a month in jail after a Flock reader wrongly linked him to a carjacking he was five miles away from. Supplemental reporting from [WebProNews](https://www.webpronews.com/san-diegos-flock-cameras-snare-the-innocent-one-mans-false-arrest-exposes-flaws-in-private-surveillance-dragnet/) pins a roughly 10% misidentification rate and at least 12 documented wrongful encounters - gunpoint stops, false arrests, even a police-dog attack.

**The "side door" to ICE is now a class action** - Audit logs are the smoking gun. Public records suggest San Jose PD granted federal agencies "side door" access, with Flock search reasons reading "fresno dea intel," "CBP," and "ICE," per [classlawgroup.com](https://www.classlawgroup.com/flock-safety-license-plate-reader-cameras-lawsuit). That has hardened into litigation: a California class action alleges Flock illegally shared plate data with federal agencies 1.6 million times, while the ACLU and EFF sued in Santa Clara County over warrantless searches.

**The pro-Flock case leans hard on San Francisco's numbers** - The counter-narrative is alive and well, led by [@a16z](https://x.com/a16z/status/2057555804556017967), who argued San Francisco's "full safety bundle" (400 license plate readers and 80 drones deployed in 2024) drove major crimes down 44% and car thefts down 54% from 2023 to 2025. [@REXShares](https://x.com/REXShares/status/2064706431127801871) echoed it this week, claiming Flock "effectively ended gang violence in stolen cars in the Bay Area" with Oakland and Berkeley auto thefts down 66% and 46%. Worth noting these efficacy stats trace largely to Flock's own marketing and investor boosters - independent verification is thin in the corpus.

**Even crime-stat believers are bailing over trust, not effectiveness** - The most telling pattern is that the cancellations are NOT driven by doubts about crime-solving. Cities like Denver credited LPRs for record-low homicide rates, yet residents still want them gone over federal data-sharing and immigration fears, per [Stateline](https://stateline.org/2026/01/08/worried-about-surveillance-states-enact-privacy-laws-and-restrict-license-plate-readers/). Flagstaff's compromise - pulling its data from Flock's national and statewide networks, sharing only with two local agencies, and cutting retention from 30 to 14 days - is emerging as the middle-path template.

KEY PATTERNS from the research:
1. The cancellation wave is real and ICE-driven, with ~50 cities now out - per [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/02/17/nx-s1-5612825/flock-contracts-canceled-immigration-survillance-concerns)
2. False matches are the most viral anti-Flock evidence; the San Diego month-in-jail case dominated discussion - per [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/)
3. Audit logs showing "ICE"/"CBP" search reasons turned the side-door allegation into a 1.6M-share class action - per [classlawgroup.com](https://www.classlawgroup.com/flock-safety-license-plate-reader-cameras-lawsuit)
4. The efficacy case (SF down 44%, car thefts down 54%) is loud but sourced mostly to Flock and investors like [@a16z](https://x.com/a16z/status/2057555804556017967)
5. The fight is about trust and federal access, not whether the cameras solve crime; Flagstaff's data-firewall + 14-day retention is the compromise model - per [Stateline](https://stateline.org/2026/01/08/worried-about-surveillance-states-enact-privacy-laws-and-restrict-license-plate-readers/)
