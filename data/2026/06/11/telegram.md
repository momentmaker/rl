*Random Learning — 2026-06-11*

Three things I went down the rabbit hole on today.

*1. What AlphaFold has actually delivered (and what it hasn't)*
The honest read on AI drug discovery. AlphaFold's genuine, shipped win is huge but narrow: it collapsed protein-structure prediction from years to seconds and is now used by 3M+ researchers in 190+ countries. The hype is everything stacked on top. DeepMind's drug spinoff Isomorphic Labs just raised a $2.1B round (the largest in AI-pharma history), and its new IsoDDE engine reportedly more than doubled AlphaFold 3's accuracy - one Columbia scientist called it "on the scale of an AlphaFold 4." But the load-bearing fact: no human patient has been dosed with an Isomorphic-designed compound, and Demis Hassabis quietly slipped the first-trial timeline from end-2025 to end-2026. The deepest critique isn't about competence - it's that protein structure was rarely the rate-limiting step in drug discovery anyway; the ~85% clinical failure rate comes from bad target choice and human toxicity, neither of which AlphaFold touches. New flashpoint: IsoDDE is fully proprietary with a "scant" technical report, breaking the open-science deal that made AlphaFold beloved.

*2. The Flock Safety surveillance backlash*
Flock's automatic license-plate-reader cameras are all over US towns - and 2026 is the year the revolt went mainstream. At least 50 cities have canceled contracts, deactivated cameras, or rejected proposals, most in the last three months. The trigger is federal access: audit logs showed search reasons reading "ICE" and "CBP," and a California class action now alleges Flock illegally shared plate data with federal agencies 1.6 million times. The viral skeptic's exhibit A is a San Diego man who spent a month in jail after a camera wrongly tied him to a carjacking five miles away; reporting pins a ~10% misidentification rate. The counter-case is loud - a16z credits San Francisco's camera-and-drone bundle with major crime down 44% - but those efficacy numbers trace mostly to Flock's own marketing. The most telling pattern: even cities that credit the cameras for low crime are pulling out anyway, over trust and federal data-sharing, not effectiveness.

*3. Airlines are banks with planes attached*
The reframe that explains modern aviation: the frequent-flyer program is worth more than the airline. Delta's SkyMiles was valued around $31B in 2026, ahead of AAdvantage and MileagePlus - at points exceeding the carriers' own market caps. The cash comes from selling miles to banks, not seats: Delta took in $8B+ from Amex last year (about 1.4x its operating income), and 57% of US miles now come from credit-card spend, not flying. The clincher - when travel cratered in 2020, Delta borrowed $9B against SkyMiles as collateral; lenders valued the miles ledger over the planes. Spirit is the counterexample that proves the rule: it ceased operations May 2, the first major US airline in 25 years to fail on financials, with no loyalty bank to lean on, a failed $500M bailout, and a war-driven fuel spike. Polymarket now gives Frontier, the next ultra-low-cost carrier, a 20% shot at bankruptcy.
