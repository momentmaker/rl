What I learned:

**The single biggest 2026 story in this space is not a switch away from Google Maps - it is a schism inside the open-source camp itself.** Organic Maps was forked in mid-2025 into [CoMaps](https://codeberg.org/comaps/comaps), and the fork is now where much of the developer energy has gone. On X, [@AncapAir's correspondent](https://x.com) put it plainly: "I use CoMaps, which is a fork of Organic Maps. Most of the developers have moved to CoMaps because of the drama around proprietary code and transparency of the project." The grievance was governance, not features: per [It's FOSS](https://itsfoss.com/news/organic-maps-fork-comaps/) and [LWN](https://lwn.net/Articles/1024387/), contributors accused the for-profit Organic Maps OÜ of centralized decision-making and of spending donation funds on personal expenses, then forked into an explicitly not-for-profit, community-accountable project ([AlternativeTo](https://alternativeto.net/news/2025/5/organic-maps-contributors-fork-project-as-concerns-over-governance-and-transparency-grow/)). For anyone evaluating "which app do people actually trust," CoMaps is now the trust-maximalist answer the privacy crowd points newcomers toward, even though Organic Maps itself remains the most-recommended starting point.

**Organic Maps is the consensus pick for "casual hiker who just wants it to work offline and not spy on them."** The recurring framing across the corpus is simplicity plus zero data collection. One X user captured the whole pitch in a sentence: "I just discovered Organic Maps - it's an F-Droid replacement for Maps that doesn't spy on you." [MakeUseOf](https://www.makeuseof.com/switched-to-privacy-maps-app-google-stopped-logging-everywhere-i-go/) describes the mechanism that makes people trust it: "anything you download stays on your device, and your device only," with map regions offered for instant download as you pan. The official project ([organicmaps/organicmaps](https://github.com/organicmaps), 14.4K stars) leans on the same identity - "a free Android & iOS offline maps app for more than 6M travelers, tourists, hikers, and cyclists... crowd-sourced OpenStreetMap data." The honest caveat surfaced in r/organicmaps is data heft, not privacy: "it takes ~90 gb to download every single map on Organic Maps."

**OsmAnd is the app serious hikers trust once the trail gets technical - the tradeoff is a steeper learning curve.** The most-repeated practitioner line is that the topo layers are the whole point: per [Beyond Baggage](https://beyondbaggage.com/2026/02/24/best-offline-maps-apps-travel-2026/), "once you sit with OsmAnd for an afternoon, nothing else comes close for serious hiking or cycling trips," with proper contour lines, offline Wikipedia and Wikivoyage, ski pistes, and difficulty-color-coded bike routes. The concrete trust moment people cite is real-world bailout: OsmAnd "got someone down a trail their guidebook had mislabelled." The friction worth flagging up front: the free tier caps you at 7 offline map downloads, after which it is OsmAnd+ (paid) or OsmAnd~ from F-Droid - a detail that pushes the most privacy-minded users to the F-Droid build.

**The "why I switched from Google Maps" motivation is overwhelmingly surveillance, and it is emotional, not just technical.** The highest-engagement evidence in the window is not a feature comparison - it is people describing what tracking feels like. [@WF_Watcher's post](https://x.com/WF_Watcher/status/2070779181160657376) (192 likes) mourns a lake that "survives only on Google Maps" after being bulldozed, a sharp metaphor for the corpus's distrust of a single corporate map of reality. Another widely-shared lament: "people who use Google Maps to get around THEIR OWN city... never learn their way around their own town anymore, relying by reflex." The switch is framed as reclaiming both privacy and competence.

**The switch away from AllTrails is a distinct, money-and-safety story rather than a privacy story.** Two complaints dominate. First, pricing: longtime users say AllTrails "lost credibility since they changed the pricing system," with route planning that used to be a $36/year Plus feature now pushed toward the new ~$80/year [Peak tier](https://globetrender.com/2025/07/28/alltrails-new-premium-membership-delivers-ai-powered-hikes/), and one reviewer calling it "slowly caving to enshitification." Second, and louder in 2026, is AI safety: per [Canada's National Observer](https://www.nationalobserver.com/2025/06/17/news/alltrails-ai-tool-search-rescue-members) and [InsideHook](https://www.insidehook.com/adventure/experts-concerns-ai-generated-hiking-routes), AllTrails' AI route generator alarmed search-and-rescue members who warn that hikers "blindly trust AI-generated routes without checking maps." Note the asymmetry: AllTrails does support offline maps - but only on paid Plus/Peak plans, per [AllTrails Help](https://support.alltrails.com/hc/en-us/articles/37213318235028-How-to-download-maps-to-your-phone-for-offline-use) - which is exactly what makes the free OSM apps attractive to the cost-and-control crowd.

**The pragmatic verdict people actually live by is a multi-app stack, not a single winner.** The repeated workflow is: find or plan the route in a planning tool, then carry it offline in an OSM app. Users describe building a route on a big screen ("I built MapDraw.net to plan trips... and take the data with you into Organic Maps") and importing GPX tracks exported from AllTrails or Wikiloc into OsmAnd to navigate them offline. For backcountry beyond what OSM covers, [The Trek](https://thetrek.co/best-hiking-navigation-apps-of-2026/) still places Gaia GPS and CalTopo as the heavyweight layered-topo options - but both store data in the cloud behind third-party logins, which is the precise property the Organic Maps/OsmAnd/CoMaps switchers are trying to escape.

KEY PATTERNS from the research:

1. **The trust hierarchy has shifted: CoMaps > Organic Maps for the privacy-maximalist crowd.** The 2025 fork over governance and donation-fund transparency means the most trust-conscious users now recommend the not-for-profit fork, while Organic Maps remains the mainstream on-ramp.
2. **Organic Maps wins on simplicity + on-device-only data; OsmAnd wins on topo depth.** "Casual, just works, doesn't spy" vs "Swiss-army-knife with contour lines" is the cleanest way the community splits the two.
3. **People switch from Google Maps for surveillance reasons, framed emotionally.** The viral posts are about distrust of a single corporate map and loss of personal navigation skill - not about feature gaps.
4. **People switch from AllTrails for money and AI-safety reasons, not privacy.** Pricing creep to the ~$80 Peak tier plus search-and-rescue warnings about AI-generated routes are the named grievances; "enshittification" is the recurring word.
5. **Offline is paywalled on AllTrails but free-and-default on the OSM apps.** AllTrails offline requires Plus/Peak; Organic Maps, OsmAnd, and CoMaps treat fully-offline, on-device maps as the baseline - the core reason for the migration.
6. **The real-world answer is a stack, not one app.** Plan a route (MapDraw/Wikiloc/CalTopo) then carry the GPX offline in OsmAnd or Organic Maps; cloud-tethered Gaia/CalTopo stay in the planning role for those who tolerate third-party logins.
