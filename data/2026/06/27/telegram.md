*Random Learning — 2026-06-27*

Three things I dug into today, from the last 30 days of what people are saying.

*1. Functional ultrasound (fUS) brain imaging — fMRI's faster, palm-sized challenger.*
The loudest signal this month is commercial, not academic: Aleph Neuro launched what it calls the highest-resolution non-invasive 3D brain images ever taken from outside the skull, built on Butterfly Network's "ultrasound-on-chip" plus FDA-approved microbubbles — "mm-resolution and wide brain coverage, in a device that fits in the palm of your hand." Where fUS beats fMRI, per practitioners: sensitivity and speed (~100µm, ~400ms sampling vs fMRI's ~10-second hemodynamic lag that caps it near 0.1 Hz), plus portability and bedside access — you can image neonates and patients mid-surgery. The strongest proof point is intra-operative: fUS during awake brain surgery mapping language areas in real time, distinguishing tumor from healthy vasculature, which pre-op fMRI can't. The honest limits researchers admit: the skull absorbs the signal (cerebellum, temporal, auditory and insular regions stay hard; full transcranial volumetric fUS isn't proven), and because it reads blood flow it's still indirect — "spatially rich but slow vs electrodes." The real 2026 shift is fUS-as-BCI, not fUS-as-scanner: Forest Neurotech is shipping a key-fob-sized imager+neuromodulator, and Caltech has decoded movement intentions in a closed loop with no implant — a middle ground between scalp EEG and Neuralink electrodes.

*2. "Presence layers" put live human company back on personal websites.*
The pitch is emotional, not technical: the web is "full of content but empty of people." TownSquare — the tool everyone's pointing at — renders visitors as little walking stick figures at the bottom of your page (orange is you, black is people on the same page, grey is people elsewhere on the site), so a static personal site can "feel inhabited." What makes it palatable is what it refuses to do: no accounts, no profiles, no cookies, no analytics, and chat that vanishes when people leave — "a single WebSocket broadcasting a number and a path." That's the pre-emptive answer to "isn't this creepy?" The best evidence visitors enjoy it: the Hacker News crush — when it hit the front page "the town completely overflowed," breaking under load, a popularity problem, not rejection. The "why now" is the 2026 personal-website revival (makers calling it "the Year of My Website"): moving off algorithmic feeds toward self-owned spaces, with human connection as the thing a feed can't offer. Honest caveat: it only feels alive with simultaneous traffic — on a quiet blog you're mostly the one orange figure.

*3. Offline hiking maps: the trust hierarchy quietly reshuffled in 2026.*
The biggest story isn't fleeing Google Maps — it's a schism inside the open-source camp. Organic Maps was forked in 2025 into CoMaps after contributors accused the for-profit parent of centralized control and misspending donation funds; most developer energy moved to the not-for-profit fork. So the answer the privacy crowd now hands newcomers is CoMaps, even though Organic Maps stays the mainstream on-ramp ("an F-Droid replacement for Maps that doesn't spy on you" — everything stays on your device). For technical terrain, OsmAnd is the one serious hikers trust — contour lines, offline Wikipedia/Wikivoyage — at the cost of a steep learning curve and a 7-download free cap. People switch *from Google Maps* for surveillance reasons (distrust of a single corporate map; losing the ability to navigate your own town). People switch *from AllTrails* for different reasons: pricing creep toward an ~$80/year Peak tier, and — louder in 2026 — search-and-rescue warnings that hikers "blindly trust AI-generated routes without checking maps." The asymmetry that drives migration: AllTrails paywalls offline maps; the OSM apps treat fully-offline, on-device maps as the baseline. The verdict people live by is a stack, not one app: plan the route somewhere (Wikiloc, CalTopo), then carry the GPX offline in OsmAnd or Organic Maps.
