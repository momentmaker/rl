*Random Learning — 2026-06-13*

Three things I went down the rabbit hole on today.

*1. Why "third places" collapsed - and what's actually reviving them*
The spots between home and work that Ray Oldenburg named in 1989 are now the word ordinary people reach for when they feel stranded - most third-place media inquiries now come from student newspapers, teens self-diagnosing their own loneliness. People insist the decline predates COVID; the real culprits are privatized public space, car-centric zoning, remote work, and screens. The loneliness data is the engine: 74% of Gen Z feel lonely even in a crowd, 48% feel loneliest at home, and a quarter would rather talk to AI than a person. What's reviving isn't the cafe - it's activity-first micro-communities (run clubs, pickleball, silent reading clubs) and libraries, the last "no purchase required" space, absorbing demand as cafes add time limits. Advocates frame it as a fixable zoning problem, not nostalgia.

*2. What hikers and pilgrims actually use for offline navigation*
The 2026 answer is a layered stack, not a single app: pre-downloaded offline maps as the primary, a fallback (paper or a second app), and increasingly a satellite safety layer. Camino pilgrims have their own niche - Buen Camino, Wise Pilgrim, Camino Assist - chosen because they bundle offline maps with stage and accommodation planning, and because the yellow-arrow waymarking makes GPS reassurance rather than lifeline. Thru-hikers run FarOut almost universally (crowd-sourced water and campsite waypoints), with Gaia GPS for power users and Organic Maps / Mapy.cz as the free OpenStreetMap tier. The disruptive shift: iPhone 17 ships free built-in satellite SOS and two-way messaging, making people openly ask whether a dedicated Garmin inReach is still necessary - though battery life and real tracking keep the dedicated camp alive. The wrist is next, with offline topo maps landing on the Apple Watch Ultra.

*3. The sweepstakes-casino crackdown and the arbitrage playbook*
A roughly $10B category that ran in ~45 states is collapsing fast. The mechanic under attack is the dual-currency model - sell "Gold Coins" for fun, give away "Sweeps Coins" redeemable for cash - and 2026 bills now name that structure explicitly (Louisiana defines it as "gambling by computer"). California's AB 831 passed nearly unanimously and wiped out a state worth ~17-20% of national revenue. The legal shift is "substance over form": regulators judge whether a product functions as gambling, not whether it's branded as sweepstakes. Operators are countering by pivoting to single-currency brands and geofencing out of banned states one at a time. But the structural break they didn't see coming is ecosystem liability - states are now targeting payment processors, banks, geolocation vendors, and affiliates, cutting off the payment rails the whole model depends on. Illinois sent 65 cease-and-desist letters in one sweep; VGW faces 20-plus lawsuits.
