Three things I went down the rabbit hole on today.

*1. Do mineral hot springs actually do anything?*
The most-marketed claim - "healing minerals soaking into your skin" - turns out to be the least-evidenced one. The controlled research points at heat, buoyancy, and immersion physics instead. Balneotherapy has real but soft evidence for arthritis and fibromyalgia (the 2008 Cochrane review found benefit but rated it only "silver-level"). The concrete part is a January 2026 meta-analysis: a single hot soak raises heart rate ~28 bpm and lowers blood pressure, and repeated immersion lowers resting heart rate over time - passive heat therapy as a real cardiovascular intervention, though the soak itself is an acute strain, so not free for everyone. The geology is the cleanest story: deep groundwater heated by magma or hot rock, rising through faults. Where they appear is pure plate tectonics - Iceland sits on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, ~80% of Japan's onsen lie within 100km of a plate boundary, and Ojo Caliente in New Mexico is fed by the Rio Grande Rift. Humans have sought out hot springs for at least 10,000 years; we settled the "does it feel good" question long before the science caught up.

*2. What sand really is, and why we're running out of it*
Under magnification, "sand" is a fossil museum: continental sand is 60-95% quartz leftovers, tropical sand is pulverized coral and shell, and Okinawa's famous "star sand" is literally the skeletons of single-celled foraminifera. Green beaches are olivine (gem-grade, it's peridot) eroded from lava; black beaches are volcanic glass. The twist: the world is genuinely running short of sand, but only the angular, water-broken kind that concrete needs. Desert sand is wind-polished too round and smooth to bond with cement, which is why Saudi Arabia - surrounded by sand seas - imports construction sand from Australia. Sand is now the second-most-extracted solid material on Earth after water (~50 billion tonnes a year, per a May 2026 UNEP report), and chasing scarce river sand has spawned violent "sand mafias" tied to hundreds of deaths a year, plus accelerating coastal erosion. The beautiful macro photo and the geopolitical fight are the same story at two zoom levels.

*3. How Japanese konbini conquered convenience*
The news peg is the May 18 death of Toshifumi Suzuki at 93 - the "God of Retail" who launched Seven-Eleven in Japan in 1974 over heavy skepticism. His signature idea was Tanpin Kanri, "management by single product": an item-by-item system using real-time POS data to stock only what each individual store actually sells, which drove relentless inventory turnover and a food-first model of fresh, ready-to-eat meals restocked multiple times a day. It worked so well that "combini tourism" is now a real 2026 travel trend - searches for "7/11 Japan" spiked ~5,000% - built on cult items like the egg salad sandwich, Lawson's Karaage-kun, and FamilyMart's famichiki. The fact that surprises everyone: the Japanese licensee grew to eclipse its American parent and, in a 2005 tender offer, bought it outright - so the American 7-Eleven is Japanese-owned. There's a darker counter-narrative too, about labor and the sheer saturation: 56,000 stores nationwide, one per ~1,800 people in Tokyo.
